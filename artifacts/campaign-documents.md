# Campaign Documents: Master Template Reference

This module is the index and structural guide for every document type a campaign produces. For each category, it defines the purpose, audience, tone, structure, length, and a brief template outline. Use these as starting frameworks — adapt to the candidate, the race, and the moment.

---

## Speeches

### Stump Speech

**Purpose:** The candidate's standard campaign speech, delivered at rallies, community events, and public appearances. The foundation of the candidate's public message.
**Audience:** General voters, supporters, media.
**Tone:** Inspirational, personal, confident. More conversational than formal.
**Structure:**
1. Opening hook (personal story, local reference, or compelling question) — 30 seconds
2. Why I am running (motivation, values, biography connection) — 1 minute
3. Three-pillar platform (each pillar gets 1-2 minutes with a story or data point)
4. The contrast (what is at stake, why this race matters, how you differ from the opponent) — 1 minute
5. Call to action (vote, volunteer, donate, spread the word) — 30 seconds
6. Close (return to opening theme, leave them inspired) — 30 seconds

**Length:** 7-10 minutes for a standard stump. 3-5 minutes for a short version. 15-20 minutes for a keynote.

### Victory / Concession Speech

**Purpose:** Post-election address to supporters and the public.
**Audience:** Supporters, media, the entire community.
**Tone:** Victory: grateful, humble, unifying, forward-looking. Concession: gracious, dignified, thankful, encouraging.
**Structure (Victory):**
1. Thank the voters
2. Thank family, staff, volunteers, and supporters by name or group
3. Acknowledge the opponent
4. Restate your commitment and vision
5. Call for unity

**Structure (Concession):**
1. Congratulate the winner
2. Thank supporters, staff, family
3. Express pride in the campaign and its values
4. Encourage continued civic engagement
5. Graceful close

**Length:** 5-8 minutes.

### Issue-Specific Speech

**Purpose:** Deep-dive on a single policy area. Used for policy rollouts, themed events, and endorsement settings.
**Audience:** Issue-engaged voters, advocacy groups, media.
**Tone:** Substantive, authoritative, passionate.
**Structure:**
1. Why this issue matters now (local data or personal story)
2. The problem (specific, documented, human)
3. The plan (3-5 concrete proposals)
4. How it will be funded/implemented
5. The vision (what success looks like)
6. Call to action

**Length:** 10-15 minutes.

## Written Communications

### Press Release

**Purpose:** Formal announcement to media. Endorsements, policy announcements, events, milestones, responses.
**Audience:** Reporters, editors, news producers.
**Tone:** Factual, newsy, quotable. Written in inverted pyramid style (most important information first).
**Structure:**
1. FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE + date
2. Headline (active voice, present tense)
3. Dateline + lead paragraph (who, what, when, where, why)
4. Supporting quote from candidate
5. Context and background paragraph
6. Additional quotes (endorser, supporter)
7. Boilerplate (candidate bio, election date, website)
8. Media contact information

**Length:** 300-500 words. One page preferred.

### Op-Ed

**Purpose:** Opinion piece under the candidate's byline for local newspapers or online publications.
**Audience:** Newspaper readers, opinion page followers, engaged voters.
**Tone:** Personal, authoritative, persuasive. Written in the candidate's voice.
**Structure:**
1. Hook (current event, personal experience, or provocative observation)
2. Thesis (clear statement of position)
3. Evidence (3 supporting arguments with facts, stories, or data)
4. Preemptive rebuttal (address the strongest counterargument)
5. Call to action or vision statement
6. Closing that echoes the opening

**Length:** 600-800 words (check publication guidelines).

### Letter to the Editor

**Purpose:** Short public statement responding to news coverage or raising a campaign issue.
**Audience:** Newspaper readers, community members.
**Tone:** Concise, clear, personal. Can be written by the candidate or supporters.
**Structure:**
1. Reference the article or issue you are responding to
2. State your position or correction
3. Support with one specific fact or personal experience
4. Close with a call to action or forward-looking statement

**Length:** 150-250 words (check publication guidelines).

## Fundraising Documents

### Fundraising Email

**Purpose:** Solicit online donations.
**Audience:** Donor list (prospects and existing donors).
**Tone:** Urgent (but not panicked), personal, direct. The candidate is talking to you, not at you.
**Structure:**
1. Subject line (specific, intriguing, or urgent)
2. Personal opening (one sentence)
3. The why now (deadline, opponent action, milestone, matching gift)
4. The impact (what the money will do, specifically)
5. The ask (specific dollar amount with a donate link)
6. P.S. line (restates the ask — many readers skip to the P.S.)

**Length:** 200-400 words. Shorter for follow-ups and deadline reminders.

### Call Time Sheet

**Purpose:** Structured document for the candidate's fundraising phone calls.
**Audience:** The candidate (internal document).
**Tone:** Brief, factual, actionable.
**Structure per prospect:**
1. Name, phone number
2. Relationship to candidate
3. Giving history (past donations to this campaign or others)
4. Personal notes (family, interests, last conversation topic)
5. Suggested ask amount
6. Notes field for outcome (pledged, declined, call back, left message)

**Length:** One prospect per row, all fields visible on one line.

### Fundraising Event Invitation

**Purpose:** Invite donors to a fundraising event (reception, dinner, house party).
**Audience:** Prospective donors and supporters.
**Tone:** Warm, exclusive, action-oriented.
**Structure:**
1. Host name(s) and "invite you to" language
2. Event type, honoree/candidate name
3. Date, time, location
4. Contribution levels (tiered: host, sponsor, guest)
5. RSVP contact information
6. Legal disclaimer (paid for by, FEC/state disclosure)

**Length:** One page or single email.

## Voter Contact Documents

### Canvass Script

**Purpose:** Guide for volunteers knocking doors.
**Audience:** Campaign volunteers (internal).
**Tone:** Conversational, natural. Scripts should be frameworks, not rigid recitations.
**Structure:**
1. Introduction (who you are, why you are there — 2 sentences)
2. Voter ID question ("Have you thought about who you are supporting for [office]?")
3. Positive message (if supportive or undecided — 2-3 sentences on why to support the candidate)
4. Ask (vote commitment, volunteer sign-up, yard sign)
5. Thank and close
6. If not supportive: thank them, move on. Do not argue.

**Length:** Half a page. Must be readable at a glance while standing at a door.

### Phone Script

**Purpose:** Guide for phone bank volunteers.
**Audience:** Campaign volunteers (internal).
**Tone:** Friendly, brief. Phone calls are shorter than door conversations.
**Structure:**
1. Introduction and ask for the right person (2 sentences)
2. Voter ID question
3. Brief message (1-2 sentences)
4. Ask (vote commitment or event attendance)
5. Thank and close

**Length:** Quarter page. Volunteers should spend 90% of the call listening, not reading.

### Text Message Templates

**Purpose:** Peer-to-peer or broadcast text campaigns.
**Audience:** Voters (via volunteers or campaign platform).
**Tone:** Brief, personal, mobile-native. No formal language.
**Structure:** One message per text. Include: who you are, why you are texting, the ask, and a link if needed.
**Length:** 160 characters for SMS, up to 300 for MMS with an image.

### Direct Mail Piece

**Purpose:** Physical mail sent to targeted voter households.
**Audience:** Targeted voter segments.
**Tone:** Varies by purpose — positive bio piece, issue contrast, GOTV reminder.
**Structure (positive bio piece):**
1. Front: Candidate photo, name, office, tagline
2. Inside: Brief bio, 3 key priorities with one sentence each
3. Back: Endorsements, election date, polling location info
**Structure (contrast piece):**
1. Front: Stark visual contrast (candidate vs. opponent on a key issue)
2. Inside: Side-by-side comparison on 3-4 issues with citations
3. Back: "The choice is clear" close, election information

**Length:** Standard 8.5x11 or 6x9 postcard format. Every word must earn its space.

## Digital Documents

### Website Content

**Purpose:** The campaign's central information hub.
**Audience:** Voters, donors, volunteers, media.
**Tone:** Professional, welcoming, action-oriented.
**Key pages:**
- Home: Hero image, candidate name, tagline, three calls to action (donate, volunteer, learn more)
- About: Candidate bio (400-600 words), photo gallery
- Issues: One page per major issue (200-400 words each)
- Endorsements: Organized by category with quotes
- Events: Calendar with sign-up links
- Donate: Simple, mobile-friendly donation form
- Volunteer: Sign-up form with shift options
- Contact: Email, phone, social media links
- Media: Press releases, press kit, high-res photos

### Social Media Content Calendar

**Purpose:** Plan and schedule social media posts across platforms.
**Audience:** Campaign digital team (internal).
**Tone:** Varies by platform and post type.
**Structure:** Weekly grid with:
- Date and day of week
- Platform (Facebook, Instagram, X, TikTok)
- Post type (photo, video, graphic, text, story, reel)
- Copy (full text of post)
- Visual (description or link to asset)
- Call to action
- Hashtags
- Approval status

## Compliance Documents

### Campaign Finance Report

**Purpose:** Required disclosure of contributions received and expenditures made.
**Audience:** Election commission, public record.
**Tone:** Factual, complete, accurate. No narrative — pure data.
**Structure:** Per jurisdiction requirements. Typically includes:
- Itemized contributions (name, address, employer, amount, date)
- Unitemized contributions (aggregate totals below itemization threshold)
- Itemized expenditures (payee, purpose, amount, date)
- Cash on hand
- Debts and obligations

**Timing:** File by every deadline. Late filings generate fines and bad press.

### Disclaimer Language

**Purpose:** Legal disclaimers required on all campaign communications.
**Structure:** "Paid for by [Committee Name]" at minimum. Many jurisdictions require additional language (treasurer name, "not authorized by any candidate" for independent expenditures, etc.).
**Placement:** On every piece of printed material, every email, every digital ad, every yard sign. Check jurisdiction-specific rules for size, placement, and language requirements.

## Strategy Documents

### Campaign Plan

**Purpose:** The comprehensive strategic document guiding the entire campaign.
**Audience:** Campaign manager, senior staff, candidate (internal, confidential).
**Tone:** Analytical, strategic, honest.
**Structure:**
1. Race overview (district, office, political environment)
2. Path to victory (vote goal, target precincts, win number)
3. Candidate assessment (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats)
4. Opponent assessment
5. Message framework
6. Voter targeting strategy
7. Field plan
8. Communications plan
9. Fundraising plan
10. Budget
11. Timeline and milestones

**Length:** 15-30 pages.

### Opposition Research Book

**Purpose:** Comprehensive file on the opponent's record, statements, and vulnerabilities.
**Audience:** Campaign manager, communications director, debate prep team (internal, highly confidential).
**Tone:** Factual, documented, sourced. Every claim must have a citation.
**Structure:**
1. Biographical profile
2. Political record (votes, statements, positions)
3. Financial record (personal finances, campaign finance, business dealings)
4. Legal record (lawsuits, violations, complaints)
5. Public statements (quotes organized by topic)
6. Vulnerabilities (with supporting evidence)
7. Strengths (know where the opponent is strong to avoid unforced errors)

**Length:** 20-50+ pages depending on the opponent's record.

## Event Documents

### Event Briefing Memo

**Purpose:** Prepare the candidate for every event. See the scheduling-advance module for the full template.
**Audience:** Candidate (internal).
**Tone:** Brief, practical, actionable.
**Length:** 1 page per event.

### Event Run of Show

**Purpose:** Minute-by-minute timeline for complex events (rallies, fundraisers, launches).
**Audience:** Event staff, AV team, volunteers (internal).
**Tone:** Precise, sequential.
**Structure:**
- Time — Action — Responsible person — Notes
- Include setup, doors open, program start, each speaker/segment with duration, program end, teardown

**Length:** 1-2 pages.

### Post-Event Report

**Purpose:** Document what happened at the event for campaign records and follow-up.
**Audience:** Campaign manager, data team (internal).
**Structure:**
1. Event name, date, location
2. Attendance (actual vs. expected)
3. Funds raised (if fundraiser)
4. Contacts collected (number of sign-ins)
5. Media present and coverage received
6. Notable conversations or commitments
7. Follow-up items with owners and deadlines
8. What worked, what to improve

**Length:** Half page to one page.

## Document Management Rules

1. **Version control:** Every document has a date in the filename or header. Never edit without updating the date.
2. **Confidentiality:** Mark internal documents CONFIDENTIAL. Opposition research is HIGHLY CONFIDENTIAL. Never email opposition research to anyone outside senior staff.
3. **Approval chain:** Press releases, paid media, and policy statements require candidate or campaign manager approval before distribution.
4. **Storage:** Keep all documents in a centralized, backed-up location accessible to authorized staff. Use consistent folder structure.
5. **Legal hold:** Preserve all campaign documents for the period required by your jurisdiction's record retention laws (typically 3-5 years after the election).
