# Michigan Local Election Rules (Detailed)

> **STALENESS WARNING:** This reference was written in April 2026. Michigan local election
> rules vary by municipality type (city, township, village) and by individual charter.
> Verify current local requirements with the appropriate county clerk or city clerk
> before filing.

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This document is for educational and informational purposes
> only. It does not constitute legal advice. Candidates should consult a qualified election
> law attorney or the relevant local election authority for guidance specific to their
> situation.

## Overview

Michigan has 83 counties, 275 cities, 1,240 townships, and 255 villages. Local
election rules are governed by the Michigan Election Law (MCL 168), individual
city charters, and township statutes. Most Michigan cities hold **nonpartisan**
elections, while county and township elections are **partisan**.

## City of Detroit

### Government Structure

| Office | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Mayor | 4-year term, nonpartisan |
| City Council (9 members) | 7 district + 2 at-large, 4-year terms |
| City Clerk | Elected, 4-year term |
| Election type | Nonpartisan primary and general |
| Election timing | Odd-numbered years |

### Detroit Election Rules
- [ ] Nonpartisan elections in odd-numbered years
- [ ] Primary: all candidates run; top two per seat advance to general
- [ ] Campaign finance reports filed with Wayne County Clerk and state
- [ ] State contribution limits apply
- [ ] Detroit Ethics Ordinance imposes additional restrictions on city officials

### Detroit-Specific Notes
- Detroit has a strong-mayor form of government
- City Council redistricted after each census
- Police commissioner elections also held (Board of Police Commissioners)
- School board: Detroit has a mix of appointed and elected oversight (see below)

## City of Ann Arbor

### Government Structure

| Office | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Mayor | 4-year term, nonpartisan |
| City Council (11 members) | 5 wards, 2 per ward + 1 at-large (mayor), various terms |
| Election type | Nonpartisan primary (August), general (November) |
| Election timing | Even-numbered years (coincides with state primary/general) |

### Ann Arbor Election Rules
- [ ] Nonpartisan elections held in even-numbered years (unusual for Michigan cities)
- [ ] Primary held same day as state partisan primary (August)
- [ ] General held same day as state general (November)
- [ ] Campaign finance filed with Washtenaw County Clerk and state
- [ ] State contribution limits apply

## Nonpartisan City Elections

Most Michigan cities hold nonpartisan elections:

| Feature | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Party affiliation | Not listed on ballot |
| Primary | Nonpartisan primary (if more than 2x seats candidates file) |
| General | Top candidates from primary advance |
| Timing | Odd-numbered years (most cities); some even-year (Ann Arbor) |
| Campaign finance | State contribution limits and disclosure rules apply |

### Nonpartisan Election Checklist
- [ ] Verify election timing for your specific city
- [ ] File nominating petition with city clerk
- [ ] No party affiliation required or listed
- [ ] Register campaign committee with Bureau of Elections
- [ ] State disclosure and contribution rules apply

## School Board Elections

Michigan school board elections vary between elected and appointed boards:

### Elected School Boards (Majority of Districts)

| Detail | Rule |
|--------|------|
| Election type | Nonpartisan |
| Term length | 4 or 6 years (varies by district) |
| Board size | 7 members (typically) |
| Filing | Nominating petition (typically 20-40 signatures) |
| Election timing | November of even-numbered years (most districts) |
| Campaign finance | State rules apply |

### Appointed School Boards

| District | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Detroit (partial) | Hybrid system with some appointed positions |
| Other districts | Some intermediate school districts have appointed boards |

### School Board Checklist
- [ ] Determine if your district has elected or appointed board
- [ ] Obtain nominating petition from school district or county clerk
- [ ] Collect required signatures from registered voters in district
- [ ] File petition by deadline (varies; typically mid-July for November election)
- [ ] Register campaign committee if raising/spending over $500
- [ ] Nonpartisan ballot -- no party affiliation

## Township Elections

Michigan townships are a significant unit of local government:

| Detail | Rule |
|--------|------|
| Governing body | Board of Trustees (supervisor, clerk, treasurer, 2-4 trustees) |
| Election type | **Partisan** (party label on ballot) |
| Term length | 4 years |
| Primary | August partisan primary |
| General | November general election |
| Filing | Filing fee ($0-$100) or petition |

### Township Checklist
- [ ] Township elections are PARTISAN (unlike most city elections)
- [ ] File with township clerk
- [ ] Register campaign committee with Bureau of Elections
- [ ] State contribution limits apply
- [ ] Partisan primary -- voters choose one party ballot

---

## Village Elections

Michigan villages have unique election procedures:

| Detail | Rule |
|--------|------|
| Governing body | Village council (president + trustees) |
| Election type | Nonpartisan (typically) |
| Election timing | March (most villages) or November |
| Filing | Nominating petition |
| Term length | 2 years (typically) |

### Village-Specific Notes
- [ ] Many villages hold elections in March (separate from other elections)
- [ ] Village caucus system may be used for nominations
- [ ] Lower signature requirements for petitions
- [ ] State campaign finance rules apply if committee formed
