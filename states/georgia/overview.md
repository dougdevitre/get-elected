# Georgia Campaign Finance Overview

> **STALENESS WARNING:** This reference reflects Georgia Code Title 21 and Georgia Government Transparency and Campaign Finance Act rules as of early 2025. Contribution limits in Georgia are adjusted periodically. The Georgia General Assembly meets annually and may amend campaign finance law. Always verify current requirements at [ethics.ga.gov](https://ethics.ga.gov) and [sos.ga.gov](https://sos.ga.gov).

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This is educational information, not legal advice. Georgia's majority-vote runoff system, corporate contribution rules, and dual oversight structure create unique compliance considerations. Consult a Georgia election law attorney for guidance specific to your campaign.

---

## Filing Agencies

### Dual Oversight
**Georgia Secretary of State**
- Website: [sos.ga.gov](https://sos.ga.gov)
- Administers elections, candidate qualifying, and ballot access

**Georgia Government Transparency and Campaign Finance Commission (Ethics Commission)**
- Website: [ethics.ga.gov](https://ethics.ga.gov)
- Administers campaign finance disclosure, registration, and enforcement
- Investigates complaints and assesses penalties
- Publishes campaign finance reports online

---

## Unique Features of Georgia Campaign Finance Law

1. **Majority-vote runoffs** -- if no candidate receives a majority (50%+1) in a primary or general election, a runoff is held between the top two finishers; this creates additional fundraising periods and reporting obligations
2. **Corporate contributions allowed** -- unlike federal law and many states, Georgia permits direct corporate contributions to candidates
3. **Contribution limits per election** -- primary, general, and runoff are each separate elections for limit purposes
4. **Lobbyist contribution restrictions** -- registered lobbyists face additional restrictions during the legislative session
5. **"Fair Campaign Practices Act"** -- Georgia has specific statutory provisions governing campaign conduct beyond finance

## Contribution Limits

| Donor Type | Statewide (per election) | State Senate / House (per election) | Local (per election) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------------|------------------------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Individual | **$7,600** | **$3,000** | **$2,600** | Verify current cycle; adjusted periodically |
| Corporation | **$7,600** | **$3,000** | **$2,600** | Direct corporate contributions are legal |
| PAC | **$7,600** | **$3,000** | **$2,600** | Same limits as individuals |
| Political Party (state) | **No limit** | **No limit** | **No limit** | Party committees are exempt from limits |
| Candidate to Own Campaign | **No limit** | **No limit** | **No limit** | Personal funds unlimited |
| Labor Union | **$7,600** | **$3,000** | **$2,600** | Treated same as other entities |
| Out-of-State PAC | Same as in-state | Same | Same | Must register with Ethics Commission |
| Cash (any source) | **$100 max** | **$100 max** | **$100 max** | Over $100 must be traceable |

### Election-Specific Notes
- **Primary, general, and runoff** are each separate elections -- a donor may contribute the maximum to each
- In a cycle with a primary, general, and runoff, a single donor could give up to three times the per-election limit
- **Aggregate limits:** Georgia does not impose aggregate limits on total contributions from an individual to all candidates

## Committee Registration

### Candidate Committees
- File a **Campaign Contribution Disclosure Report -- Registration** with the Ethics Commission
- Must register within 15 days of receiving contributions or making expenditures exceeding **$1,000**
- Must designate a chairperson and treasurer
- May designate a campaign depository

### Political Action Committees
- Register with the Ethics Commission before soliciting or accepting contributions
- Must file organizing documents including statement of purpose
- **Independent committees** making independent expenditures must also register and report

### Party Committees
- State, district, and county party committees register with the Ethics Commission
- Exempt from contribution limits when giving to candidates

## Ballot Access

### Major Party Candidates (Republican / Democrat)
- **Qualifying period:** A brief window (typically 3 days) in March of the election year
- **Qualifying fee:** Percentage of the annual salary of the office (typically 3%)
- **Alternative:** Candidates may qualify by paying the fee; there is no petition-in-lieu option for major party candidates
- Primary elections held in May (typically the fourth Tuesday)
- **Runoff:** If no candidate receives a majority, a runoff is held approximately 4 weeks after the primary

### Independent and Minor Party Candidates
- Must collect petition signatures: **1% of registered voters** in the district eligible to vote in the last election for that office
- Filing deadlines vary
- Minor parties must maintain ballot access through vote thresholds

### General Election Runoffs
- If no candidate receives a **majority** (50%+1) in the general election, a runoff is held
- Runoff date is set by statute (typically 4 weeks after the general, though this has been amended -- verify current timeline)
- The runoff is a separate election for contribution limit purposes

## Reporting Schedule

### Regular Reports
| Report | Due Date | Coverage |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **June 30 report** | July 15 | January 1 through June 30 |
| **December 31 report** | January 31 | July 1 through December 31 |
| **Supplemental (September 30)** | October 15 | January 1 through September 30 (election years) |

### Election-Related Reports
| Report | Due Date | Coverage |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **Pre-primary (15 days before)** | 15 days before primary | Through 18 days before primary |
| **Pre-general (15 days before)** | 15 days before general | Through 18 days before general |
| **Pre-runoff (15 days before)** | 15 days before runoff | Through 18 days before runoff |

### Special Reporting
- Contributions of **$1,000 or more** received in the period between the last pre-election report and the election must be reported within **2 business days**
- Independent expenditures of **$500 or more** must be reported within **24 hours** during the last 15 days before an election

### Itemization
- All contributions of **$101 or more** from a single source must be itemized with full contributor information
- Contributions of $100 or less may be aggregated
- All expenditures over **$101** must be itemized

## Prohibited Contributions

- Contributions exceeding the per-election limits
- **Cash contributions exceeding $100**
- Contributions in the **name of another** (straw donors)
- **Foreign national** contributions
- **Anonymous contributions exceeding $100** -- must be donated to charity or the state
- Contributions by **lobbyists** to members of the General Assembly or the Governor during the legislative session and a period surrounding it (specific blackout period applies)
- Contributions from entities not authorized under Georgia law

## Key Differences from Federal Law

| Feature | Federal | Georgia |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Individual contribution limit | $3,300/election | **$7,600/election** (statewide); lower for legislative/local |
| Corporate contributions | Prohibited | **Allowed** (up to the limit) |
| Union contributions | Prohibited (direct) | **Allowed** (up to the limit) |
| Runoff elections | No federal runoffs | **Majority-vote runoffs** (separate election for limits) |
| Party-to-candidate limit | Coordinated expenditure limits | **No limit** |
| Cash cap | $100 | **$100** (same) |
| Reporting frequency | Quarterly/monthly | Semi-annual + pre-election (15 days before) |
| Public financing | Presidential | **None** |
| Lobbyist restrictions | No session blackout | **Session blackout for legislative/Governor** |

## Local Rules Notes

- **County and municipal candidates** generally follow state campaign finance law and file with the Ethics Commission or the local election superintendent
- **City of Atlanta** follows state law for campaign finance (no separate city limits beyond state law, though the Atlanta city ethics code has related provisions)
- **Fulton County, DeKalb County, Cobb County** and other major counties follow state rules
- Some municipalities may have local ethics ordinances with gift restrictions or pay-to-play provisions that interact with campaign finance
- Local qualifying typically occurs through the municipal government or county election office
- **Judicial candidates** are subject to the Judicial Qualifications Commission rules in addition to campaign finance law
