# Illinois Local Office Election Rules

> **STALENESS WARNING:** This reference was written in April 2026. Local election rules
> in Illinois vary by jurisdiction type and are subject to change through legislation,
> referenda, and home rule ordinances. Always verify current rules with the relevant
> election authority.

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This document is for educational and informational purposes
> only. It does not constitute legal advice. Campaigns should consult a qualified election
> law attorney or the relevant election authority for guidance specific to their situation.

---

## Overview

Illinois local elections are governed by the Illinois Election Code (10 ILCS 5) and the
Illinois Municipal Code, supplemented by home rule authority for qualifying municipalities.
The most distinctive jurisdiction is the **City of Chicago**, which has its own Board of
Election Commissioners separate from the Cook County system. Illinois holds local
elections in **consolidated elections** on the first Tuesday in April of odd-numbered
years, though some local offices are on even-year ballots.

## Chicago

### Election Authority

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Authority** | Chicago Board of Election Commissioners |
| **Website** | https://chicagoelections.gov |
| **Phone** | (312) 269-7900 |

### Key Rules

- **Nonpartisan elections:** Chicago municipal elections are nonpartisan (no party labels
  on the ballot).
- **Election timing:** Municipal elections held in **February** of odd-numbered years
  (aldermanic, mayoral). Runoffs held in **April** if no candidate receives a majority.
- **Mayor:** Elected citywide. Four-year term. No term limits (as of this writing).
- **City Council:** 50 alderpersons elected from wards. Four-year terms. No term limits.
- **City Clerk:** Elected citywide. Four-year term.
- **City Treasurer:** Elected citywide. Four-year term.

### Chicago Campaign Finance

- **State rules apply:** Illinois SBE contribution limits and reporting requirements
  apply to all Chicago candidates.
- **No additional local limits:** Chicago does not impose its own contribution limits
  beyond state law.
- **Filing:** Campaign finance reports filed with the **SBE** (state centralized system),
  not the Chicago Board of Election Commissioners.
- **Ethics ordinance:** Chicago's Municipal Code includes ethics provisions (gift bans,
  lobbying registration) that supplement state campaign finance law.
- **Board of Ethics:** The City of Chicago Board of Ethics oversees ethics compliance
  but does not regulate campaign contributions.

### Chicago Board of Election Commissioners vs. Cook County

Chicago has its **own** Board of Election Commissioners, separate from the Cook County
Clerk's election operations:

| Function | Chicago Board | Cook County Clerk |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Voter registration (Chicago) | Yes | No |
| Municipal elections (Chicago) | Yes | No |
| State/federal elections (Chicago) | Yes | No |
| Suburban Cook County elections | No | Yes |
| Petition filing (Chicago candidates) | Yes | No |

## Cook County (Suburban)

### Election Authority

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Authority** | Cook County Clerk (Elections Division) |
| **Website** | https://www.cookcountyclerkil.gov/elections |

### Key Rules

- **County offices:** Cook County Board President and 17 County Commissioners are
  elected in **partisan** elections in **even-numbered years**.
- **State's Attorney, Clerk, Sheriff, Treasurer, Assessor:** Elected countywide in
  partisan elections (even years).
- **Suburban municipal elections:** Administered by the Cook County Clerk.
- **Campaign finance:** State SBE rules apply. Cook County does not impose additional
  local contribution limits.

### Cook County Commissioner Districts

- 17 single-member districts.
- Commissioners serve four-year terms.
- Elections are staggered (some seats up in presidential years, others in gubernatorial
  years).

## Home Rule Municipality Variations

Illinois grants **home rule** authority to any municipality with a population over
25,000 (or that adopts home rule by referendum). Home rule municipalities have broad
powers, but in practice:

- **Campaign finance:** Home rule municipalities generally do **not** impose their own
  contribution limits. State SBE limits and reporting requirements apply uniformly.
- **Election procedures:** Must follow state Election Code for ballot access, petition
  filing, and election administration.
- **Ethics ordinances:** Home rule cities may adopt their own ethics ordinances (gift
  bans, lobbying rules, financial disclosure) that go beyond state requirements.
- **Term limits:** May be imposed by local referendum or ordinance.

### Notable Home Rule Cities

| City | Population | Notable Features |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Chicago | ~2,700,000 | Own election board; separate ethics ordinance |
| Aurora | ~180,000 | Mayor-council form; aldermanic wards |
| Naperville | ~150,000 | Council-manager form |
| Joliet | ~150,000 | Mayor-council form |

| Springfield | ~115,000 | State capital; mayor-aldermanic form |
| Elgin | ~115,000 | Council-manager form |
| Peoria | ~115,000 | Mayor-council-manager form |

## Township Elections

Illinois townships are an important layer of local government in many counties:

- **Election timing:** Consolidated election (first Tuesday in April, odd years).
- **Offices:** Township Supervisor, Township Clerk, Assessor (in some counties),
  Highway Commissioner, Trustees (typically 4).
- **Nonpartisan or partisan:** Township elections are **partisan** in most of Illinois.
  Candidates file nominating petitions with party identification.
- **Petition signatures:** Vary by township population. Typically:
  - Supervisor: 50-200 signatures
  - Other township offices: 25-100 signatures
- **Campaign finance:** State SBE rules apply. Many township campaigns are small enough
  that they may not trigger committee registration ($5,000 threshold).

## Village and City Elections (Non-Home Rule)

General-law (non-home rule) municipalities follow state Election Code procedures:

### Village Board Elections

- **Election timing:** Consolidated election (April, odd years).
- **Offices:** Village President (or Mayor), Board of Trustees (typically 6, serving
  overlapping 4-year terms).
- **Nonpartisan:** Most village elections are nonpartisan, though candidates may organize
  on local "party" or slate tickets.
- **Petition signatures:** Typically 25-100 depending on population.
- **Campaign finance:** State SBE rules apply.
- **Filing:** Petitions filed with the local election authority (municipal clerk or
  county clerk, depending on jurisdiction).

### City Council Elections

- Similar to village elections but may have ward-based systems.
- Aldermen/council members serve 4-year terms in most cities.
- Mayor may be elected directly or selected from among council members.

## School Board Elections

Illinois school board elections have seen significant changes:

### General Rules

- **Election timing:** Consolidated election (April, odd years).
- **Nonpartisan elections** -- no party labels on ballot.
- **Filing:** Nominating petitions filed with the county clerk or local election
  authority.
- **Petition signatures:** Typically 50-200 depending on district population.

### Petition Requirements by District Size

| District Size | Minimum Signatures |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Under 1,000 registered voters | 25 |
| 1,000 - 5,000 registered voters | 50 |
| 5,000 - 10,000 registered voters | 100 |
| Over 10,000 registered voters | 200 |

### Chicago Public Schools (CPS)

Chicago Public Schools transitioned to a **partially elected** school board. Key details:

- Prior to recent legislation, the CPS board was entirely appointed by the mayor.
- Under the new structure, some members are elected from districts and some are
  appointed (verify current structure as implementation is ongoing).
- Campaign finance: State SBE rules apply to elected board races.
- Filing: Through the Chicago Board of Election Commissioners.

## Special District Elections

Illinois has numerous special districts with elected boards:

| District Type | Election Timing | Typical Board Size |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Park District | April, odd years | 5-7 commissioners |
| Library District | April, odd years | 5-7 trustees |
| Fire Protection District | April, odd years | 3-5 trustees |
| Sanitary District | Even years (some) or odd years | Varies |
| Community College District | April, odd years | 7 trustees |
| Forest Preserve District | Even years | Varies by county |

- **Petition signatures:** Typically 25-100 depending on district population.
- **Nonpartisan** elections for most special districts.
- **Campaign finance:** State SBE rules apply if the campaign exceeds the $5,000
  committee registration threshold.

## Judicial Elections

- **Circuit Court judges:** Elected in **partisan** elections in **even-numbered years**
  (retained through nonpartisan retention elections).
- **Subcircuit judges:** Elected from subcircuit districts in partisan primaries and
  general elections.
- **Appellate Court judges:** Elected in partisan elections; retained through retention
  elections.
- **Supreme Court justices:** Elected by judicial district in partisan elections;
  retained through retention elections.
- **Campaign finance:** State SBE rules apply. No special judicial contribution limits
  in Illinois (unlike some states).

---

## Campaign Finance Rules for All Local Offices

Regardless of office type, the following state rules apply:

- **Contribution limits:** Standard limits ($5,800 individual, $58,400 PAC) unless
  limits-off is triggered.
- **Committee registration:** Required if the campaign receives or spends over $5,000.
- **Reporting:** Filed through the SBE centralized system (Illinois Sunshine).
- **Itemization:** $150 threshold.
- **No additional local limits** unless a home rule jurisdiction has specifically enacted
  them (rare in Illinois).
