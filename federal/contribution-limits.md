# Federal Contribution Limits

> **STALENESS WARNING:** Contribution limits are adjusted for inflation in odd-numbered years. The limits in this file reflect the **2025-2026 election cycle** as announced by the FEC. Limits for future cycles will differ. Always verify current limits at [fec.gov/help-candidates-and-committees/candidate-taking-receipts/contribution-limits/](https://www.fec.gov/help-candidates-and-committees/candidate-taking-receipts/contribution-limits/) before relying on any figure.

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This is educational information, not legal advice. Consult a campaign finance attorney or the FEC directly for guidance specific to your situation.

> **CURRENT CYCLE NOTICE:** All contribution limits and compliance information in this file must be verified via the FEC website for the current election cycle before use in campaign operations.

### Biennial Aggregate Limits

The Supreme Court struck down aggregate limits in **McCutcheon v. FEC (2014)**. There is no longer a cap on the total amount an individual can give to all federal candidates and committees combined during a two-year cycle. Each contribution is still subject to the per-recipient limits above.

## Key Rules and Concepts

### Per-Election Limits

Federal contribution limits apply **per election**, not per cycle. The **primary** and **general** elections are separate elections, each with its own limit.

- An individual can give **$3,300 to a candidate for the primary** AND **$3,300 to the same candidate for the general** = $6,600 total for the cycle
- Runoff elections, special elections, and convention nominations each count as separate elections with separate limits
- If a candidate runs unopposed in a primary, the primary is still a separate election

### Earmarking and Designation

- Donors can **designate** which election their contribution applies to (primary or general)
- If a contribution is **not designated**, it is applied to the next upcoming election
- If a candidate loses the primary, **general election contributions must be refunded**, redesignated (with donor permission), or reattributed

### In-Kind Contributions

Goods, services, or anything of value provided to a campaign (other than money) count as contributions at **fair market value** and are subject to the same limits.

Examples:
- A supporter donates office furniture worth $500 -- that is a $500 in-kind contribution
- A graphic designer creates the campaign logo for free (normally charges $2,000) -- that is a $2,000 in-kind contribution
- A volunteer's **personal services** are NOT in-kind contributions (volunteer exemption)

### Exceptions to the Volunteer Exemption
The volunteer exemption does NOT apply when:
- The volunteer uses their employer's resources (equipment, supplies, facilities) with the employer's knowledge
- A corporation or union directs an employee to volunteer (this becomes a corporate/union contribution)

## Super PAC Rules

**Super PACs (Independent Expenditure-Only Committees)** operate under fundamentally different rules:

### What Super PACs CAN Do
- Accept **unlimited contributions** from individuals, corporations, unions, other PACs, and most other entities
- Spend **unlimited amounts** on independent expenditures (ads, mail, digital, etc.) supporting or opposing federal candidates
- Raise funds from sources that cannot contribute directly to candidates (corporations, unions)

### What Super PACs CANNOT Do
- **Cannot contribute directly to any federal candidate** -- not $1, not any amount
- **Cannot coordinate** with candidates or their campaigns regarding:
  - The content of communications
  - The timing of communications
  - The targeting or distribution of communications
  - Strategic decisions about expenditures
- Cannot share non-public polling data, media plans, or strategic information with candidates

### Coordination Defined
Under FEC rules, a communication is "coordinated" if it is:
1. Paid for by someone other than the candidate
2. Made in cooperation, consultation, or concert with, or at the request or suggestion of, the candidate or their agents
3. Satisfies a "conduct" standard (e.g., the spender uses a former campaign employee, shares information with the campaign, or the candidate's agent is involved in creating the communication)

**Coordinated expenditures are treated as in-kind contributions** and are subject to contribution limits. For a Super PAC, which cannot contribute to candidates at all, coordination converts what should be an independent expenditure into an illegal contribution.

### Practical Firewall Guidance
- Campaign staff and Super PAC staff should **not communicate** about campaign strategy, messaging, or spending
- If a person leaves a campaign to work for a Super PAC (or vice versa), they should observe a **cooling-off period** (FEC rules specify 120 days for certain purposes)
- Publicly available information (speeches, social media posts, public statements) is not coordination -- Super PACs can use publicly available material freely
- Campaigns should **not signal** to Super PACs through public statements designed to convey strategic preferences (this is a gray area but has been the subject of complaints)

## Party Committee Expenditure Limits

National and state party committees have special authority to make **coordinated expenditures** on behalf of their nominees. These are separate from (and in addition to) direct contributions.

### Coordinated Party Expenditure Limits (Section 441a(d))

These limits are adjusted for inflation each cycle. Approximate 2025-2026 figures (verify at fec.gov):

| Office | Coordinated Expenditure Limit (approximate) |
|--------|---------------------------------------------|
| **U.S. Senate** | Varies by state voting-age population. Ranges from ~$120,000 (smallest states) to ~$3.5 million (California) |
| **U.S. House** | ~$60,000 - $65,000 (uniform national figure) |
| **President** | ~$30-35 million (combined national party limit) |

- Both the **national party** and the **state party** can each spend up to the limit, effectively doubling it
- State parties can **assign** their coordinated expenditure authority to the national party (and vice versa)
- Coordinated expenditures are **in addition to** direct contributions ($5,000/election from a party committee to a candidate)

### Party Independent Expenditures

Party committees can also make **unlimited independent expenditures**, but if a party makes independent expenditures for a candidate, it may **forfeit its right to make coordinated expenditures** for that candidate (and vice versa) in that election. This is known as the "Chevron problem" and party committees must choose their strategy carefully.

## Joint Fundraising

Joint fundraising allows two or more committees to raise money together at a single event or through a single solicitation, then split the proceeds according to a formula.

### How It Works

1. The participating committees establish a **Joint Fundraising Committee (JFC)** or designate one of the participating committees to act as the fundraising representative
2. The JFC files **FEC Form 1** and reports all receipts and disbursements
3. A **Joint Fundraising Agreement** specifies how funds are allocated among participants (typically, contributions fill up each participant in order of priority)
4. Each contributor's gift is subject to the **per-committee limits for each recipient**

### Common Structure

A typical presidential JFC might include:
- The candidate's campaign committee (receives first $3,300 for primary, then $3,300 for general)
- The national party committee (receives next $41,300)
- State party committees (receive up to $10,000 combined each)

This allows a single donor to write **one large check** that is then divided among multiple committees, each within its respective limit. A donor might give $100,000+ to a JFC even though no single committee receives more than its legal limit.

### Rules and Restrictions
- Each donor's total contribution to the JFC is subject to the **aggregate limits for each recipient** -- the JFC cannot accept more from a donor than the donor is allowed to give to each recipient
- The allocation formula must be written and disclosed
- All committees in the JFC are jointly and severally liable for compliance
- The JFC must report the names of all contributors and the allocation to each participating committee

## Special Source Rules

### Self-Funding (Personal Funds of the Candidate)

- A candidate may contribute or loan **unlimited personal funds** to their own campaign
- "Personal funds" includes: the candidate's assets, jointly held assets, income, and bequeathed funds
- Personal funds do NOT include funds raised by the campaign or funds belonging to other family members (unless given as a contribution within limits)
- The **"Millionaire's Amendment"** (BCRA Section 319) was struck down by the Supreme Court in **Davis v. FEC (2008)**, so there are no longer increased limits for opponents of self-funding candidates

### Family Contributions

- Family members (spouse, parents, children, siblings) are treated as **individual contributors** -- each subject to the individual limit ($3,300/election)
- A candidate's spouse can contribute up to the individual limit from their own funds, plus jointly held funds can be used by the candidate as personal funds
- Minor children can contribute from their own funds if the decision is genuinely theirs (FEC scrutinizes contributions from minors)

### LLC Contributions

- An **LLC with a single member** is attributed to that member as an individual contribution (subject to individual limits)
- An **LLC with multiple members** that has not elected corporate tax treatment is treated like a **partnership** -- contributions are attributed to the members
- An **LLC that has elected corporate tax treatment** (S-corp or C-corp) is treated as a **corporation** and **cannot contribute** to federal candidates
- See `federal/prohibited-contributions.md` for more on entity contributions

### Partnerships

- A **partnership** may contribute to federal candidates
- The contribution is attributed to **individual partners** according to either the partnership agreement's profit-sharing formula or as designated by the partnership
- Each partner's attributed share counts against that partner's individual contribution limit
- The partnership must provide the allocation breakdown to the recipient committee

## Enforcement of Limits

### What Happens If a Campaign Receives an Excessive Contribution

The campaign has **30 days** from the treasurer's receipt to resolve an excessive contribution by:

1. **Refunding** the excess amount to the donor
2. **Redesignating** the excess to another election (with written donor permission) -- e.g., moving excess primary funds to the general election account
3. **Reattributing** the excess to another person (e.g., if a couple intended to contribute jointly, reattribute half to the spouse, with written permission from both)

If not resolved within 30 days, both the campaign and the donor may be subject to enforcement action.

### Record-Keeping for Limits

Campaigns must maintain records sufficient to ensure compliance with limits. This includes:
- Tracking **aggregate contributions** from each donor across the entire election cycle
- Collecting and recording donor **name, address, occupation, and employer** for all contributions exceeding $200 in the aggregate
- Maintaining records of all redesignations and reattributions
- Tracking separate limits for primary, general, and any runoff or special elections
