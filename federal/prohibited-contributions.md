# Prohibited Contributions in Federal Elections

> **STALENESS WARNING:** Prohibited source rules are established by statute (52 U.S.C.) and FEC regulation (11 CFR). While these prohibitions are relatively stable compared to contribution limits, court decisions and new FEC advisory opinions can change their application. This reference reflects the law as of early 2025. Always verify current rules at [fec.gov](https://www.fec.gov).

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This is educational information, not legal advice. Accepting a prohibited contribution can result in civil and criminal penalties for both the donor and the campaign. Consult a campaign finance attorney for guidance specific to your situation.

> **CURRENT CYCLE NOTICE:** All compliance information in this file should be verified via the FEC website and applicable legal authorities for the current election cycle before relying on it for contribution acceptance decisions.

---

## Overview

Federal law prohibits certain sources from making contributions to federal candidates, party committees, and traditional PACs. Violations can result in civil penalties, criminal prosecution, and reputational damage. Treasurers have an affirmative obligation to screen contributions and return those from prohibited sources.

---

## Prohibited Sources

### 1. Foreign Nationals

**52 U.S.C. Section 30121 -- Strict Prohibition**

Foreign nationals may **not**:
- Make any contribution or donation of money or anything of value in connection with any federal, state, or local election
- Make any expenditure, independent expenditure, or disbursement in connection with any election
- Make any contribution or donation to a political party committee
- Act as a conduit or intermediary for any of the above

**Who is a "foreign national"?**
- A citizen of a foreign country who is not a lawful permanent resident of the United States
- A foreign government or agent of a foreign government
- A foreign political party
- A foreign corporation, partnership, or association (even if it has U.S. operations)

**Penalty:** Both the donor and any person who **knowingly solicits, accepts, or receives** a foreign national contribution face civil and criminal penalties. This is one of the most serious violations in campaign finance law.

**Important -- Foreign-owned U.S. subsidiaries:** A U.S. subsidiary of a foreign corporation may establish a PAC using funds from its U.S. employees, but **no foreign national** may participate in the PAC's decision-making regarding political contributions or expenditures. The foreign parent company cannot direct, control, or fund the PAC.

---

### 2. Federal Government Contractors

**52 U.S.C. Section 30119**

Any person or entity that has a **contract with the federal government** (or is negotiating one) may **not** make contributions to:
- Federal candidates
- Political party committees
- Traditional PACs

**Scope:**
- Applies to individuals, sole proprietors, partnerships, corporations, and other entities
- Covers contracts for goods, services, or real property
- Includes subcontractors if they have a direct contractual relationship with the federal government
- Applies for the **entire duration** of the contract (from negotiation through completion)

**What IS allowed:**
- Federal contractors **may** contribute to Super PACs (which only make independent expenditures)
- Individual employees of corporate federal contractors **may** contribute from their personal funds (the ban applies to the contracting entity, not its employees individually)
- Federal contractors **may** establish and administer a connected PAC (SSF), which collects voluntary contributions from employees

---

### 3. Corporations (Direct Contributions)

**52 U.S.C. Section 30118**

Corporations may **not** make direct contributions to:
- Federal candidates
- Political party committees

**This includes:**
- For-profit corporations (C-corps, S-corps)
- Incorporated nonprofits
- Incorporated trade associations
- LLCs that have elected corporate tax treatment

**What corporations CAN do:**
- Establish and administer a **PAC (Separate Segregated Fund / SSF)** that solicits voluntary contributions from the corporation's restricted class (executives, shareholders, employees and their families)
- Contribute **unlimited amounts** to **Super PACs**
- Make **independent expenditures** through a Super PAC
- Pay the **administrative and solicitation costs** of their connected PAC
- Engage in **internal communications** about elections with their restricted class
- Conduct **nonpartisan voter registration and GOTV** activities directed at their employees

---

### 4. National Banks

National banks (federally chartered banks) are subject to the same prohibition as corporations. They may not make direct contributions to federal candidates or party committees.

---

### 5. Labor Union Treasury Funds

**52 U.S.C. Section 30118**

Labor unions may **not** use treasury funds to make direct contributions to federal candidates or party committees.

**What unions CAN do (same structure as corporations):**
- Establish and administer a **PAC (SSF)** that solicits voluntary contributions from the union's members
- Contribute unlimited amounts to **Super PACs**
- Pay the administrative and solicitation costs of their connected PAC
- Conduct **internal communications** about elections with their members
- Conduct **nonpartisan voter registration and GOTV** among their members and their families

---

### 6. Contributions in the Name of Another (Straw Donors)

**52 U.S.C. Section 30122 -- Strict Prohibition**

It is illegal to:
- Make a contribution **in the name of another person** (straw donor)
- Knowingly permit your name to be used to disguise the true source of a contribution
- Knowingly accept a contribution made in the name of another

**Examples of violations:**
- Person A gives Person B $3,300 and asks Person B to contribute it to Candidate X -- this is an illegal straw donor contribution
- A corporation gives bonuses to employees with the understanding that they will contribute to a specific candidate
- A parent contributes in their minor child's name without the child's genuine participation

**This is a criminal offense.** The DOJ actively investigates and prosecutes straw donor schemes, which can result in felony charges, prison sentences, and substantial fines.

---

### 7. Cash Contributions Over $100

**11 CFR Section 110.4(c)**

- No campaign may accept **cash contributions exceeding $100** from any one person
- Cash contributions of $100 or less are legal but must be documented with the donor's name and reported
- This applies to physical currency only -- checks, credit cards, wire transfers, and digital payments are not "cash" for this purpose
- Money orders and cashier's checks are not considered "cash" and may be accepted in any amount (within contribution limits)

**Practical tip:** Many campaigns decline all cash contributions to avoid record-keeping complications and appearance issues.

---

### 8. Anonymous Contributions Over $50

**11 CFR Section 110.4(c)**

- Contributions over **$50** cannot be accepted if the contributor's identity is unknown
- Anonymous contributions of $50 or less may be accepted and reported as unitemized
- If the campaign cannot identify the contributor of a gift exceeding $50, it may not keep the funds -- they must be returned or disposed of (donated to charity, sent to the U.S. Treasury, or used for another lawful purpose unrelated to any federal election)

## Common Edge Cases and Frequently Asked Questions

### Lawful Permanent Residents (Green Card Holders) -- CAN Contribute

Lawful permanent residents (green card holders) are **not foreign nationals** under FECA. They **may contribute** to federal candidates, PACs, and party committees on the same terms as U.S. citizens, subject to the same contribution limits.

- They must have a valid green card (Form I-551) or other evidence of lawful permanent resident status
- Campaigns should document the donor's status if there is any question
- Conditional permanent residents (2-year green cards) also qualify

### Dual Citizens -- CAN Contribute

A person who holds **dual citizenship** (U.S. and another country) is a U.S. citizen and may contribute without restriction (subject to standard limits). Their foreign citizenship is irrelevant.

### Visa Holders (H-1B, F-1, etc.) -- CANNOT Contribute

Temporary visa holders (work visas, student visas, tourist visas) are **foreign nationals** and may **not** contribute, regardless of how long they have lived in the United States. Only lawful permanent residents and U.S. citizens may contribute.

- H-1B visa holders: **Cannot** contribute
- F-1/J-1 visa holders: **Cannot** contribute
- TPS (Temporary Protected Status): **Cannot** contribute (TPS does not confer lawful permanent resident status)
- DACA recipients: **Cannot** contribute (DACA is not lawful permanent resident status)
- Asylum seekers/refugees who have not obtained green cards: **Cannot** contribute

### LLCs -- Depends on Structure

The treatment of LLC contributions depends on the LLC's tax election:

| LLC Type | Treatment | Can Contribute to Federal Candidates? |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Single-member LLC** (no corporate election) | Attributed to the sole member as an individual contribution | Yes, subject to individual limits; counts against the member's personal limit |
| **Multi-member LLC** (no corporate election) | Treated as a partnership; contribution attributed to members | Yes, attributed to members per their ownership shares |
| **LLC with corporate tax election** (S-corp or C-corp) | Treated as a corporation | **No** -- prohibited source |

**Campaign obligation:** If you receive a contribution from an LLC, you must determine which category applies BEFORE depositing the contribution. Request documentation of the LLC's tax status.

### Partnerships -- CAN Contribute

Partnerships may contribute to federal candidates. However:

- The contribution is **attributed to individual partners**, not the partnership entity
- Each partner's share counts against that partner's **individual contribution limit**
- The partnership must provide the **allocation** among partners to the recipient campaign
- Partners can agree on any allocation, but the total attributed to any one partner cannot exceed that partner's individual limit
- A partnership with a corporate partner must ensure the corporate partner's share is not included in the contribution (or is zero)

### Sole Proprietorships -- CAN Contribute

A sole proprietorship is legally the same as the individual owner. Contributions from a sole proprietorship are treated as contributions from the individual, subject to individual limits.

### Contributions from Minors

- Minors may contribute to federal candidates if:
  1. The decision to contribute is **genuinely** the minor's own
  2. The funds contributed are **owned or controlled by the minor** (allowance, earned income, savings)
  3. The contribution is **not directed or controlled by a parent** or other adult
- The FEC scrutinizes contributions from very young children -- a $3,300 contribution from a 5-year-old will raise red flags
- There is no minimum age for making a contribution, but the genuineness requirement effectively limits contributions from very young children

### Contributions via Cryptocurrency

Per FEC Advisory Opinion 2014-02:
- Cryptocurrency contributions are treated as **in-kind contributions** (not cash or monetary)
- Subject to standard contribution limits
- Must collect donor information (name, address, occupation, employer)
- Must report the fair market value at the time of receipt
- The contributor must be verified as not a foreign national or other prohibited source
- Some campaigns decline cryptocurrency due to compliance complexity

## What to Do If You Receive a Prohibited Contribution

### Immediate Steps

1. **Do not deposit the contribution** if you know or suspect it is from a prohibited source
2. If already deposited, **return the contribution** within 30 days of the treasurer's discovery
3. Document the reason for the return
4. If you cannot identify the source of a contribution over $50, you may not keep it
5. Report the contribution and the refund on your next FEC report

### Investigation Obligation

Treasurers have an obligation to exercise **reasonable diligence** in screening contributions:

- Check contributor names against known prohibited categories
- Verify LLC and corporate status before accepting entity contributions
- Screen for foreign national status when indicators are present (foreign address, foreign bank, etc.)
- Investigate any contribution that raises red flags (unusually large, contributor unknown, unusual pattern)
- If a contribution arrives from a suspicious source, do not deposit it until you have verified its legality

### When in Doubt

If there is any question about whether a contribution is legal:
1. Do not deposit it
2. Consult a campaign finance attorney
3. Contact the FEC Info Line (1-800-424-9530) for guidance
4. If the contribution has been deposited, refund it immediately pending resolution

## Prohibited Activities Beyond Contributions

### Promise of Government Action

It is illegal to solicit contributions in exchange for a promise of official government action. This includes:
- Promising appointments, contracts, or favorable regulatory treatment in exchange for contributions
- Explicitly or implicitly conditioning government access or action on donation amounts

### Coercion

It is illegal to coerce or intimidate anyone into making a contribution, including:
- Threatening adverse employment action unless a contribution is made
- Making contributions a condition of employment or promotion
- Using physical force or threats

### Solicitation on Federal Property

It is illegal to **solicit contributions in a federal building** or on federal property (the Pendleton Act and subsequent statutes). This includes the White House, Congressional offices, and federal agency buildings.

- Exception: Virtual events hosted by a person who happens to be sitting in a federal building are a gray area -- the FEC and courts have not fully resolved this for all scenarios
- Members of Congress may not solicit contributions from their official offices
