# Federal Election Commission (FEC) Overview

> **STALENESS WARNING:** This reference reflects FEC rules and procedures as of early 2025. The FEC periodically updates forms, thresholds, and procedures. Always verify current requirements at [fec.gov](https://www.fec.gov) before taking action. Contribution limits are indexed for inflation and change every odd-numbered year.

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** This is educational information, not legal advice. Consult a campaign finance attorney or the FEC directly for guidance specific to your situation.

> **CURRENT CYCLE NOTICE:** All compliance information in this file should be verified against the FEC website ([fec.gov](https://www.fec.gov)) for the current election cycle before relying on it for filing, registration, or reporting decisions.

## What Is the FEC?

The **Federal Election Commission** is the independent regulatory agency responsible for administering and enforcing federal campaign finance law. It was created by the Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA) Amendments of 1974 and began operating in 1975.

### Structure

- **Six commissioners** appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate
- No more than **three commissioners** may belong to the same political party
- Commissioners serve **staggered six-year terms**
- **Four votes** are required for any official action (investigations, advisory opinions, rulemaking, enforcement)
- The Chair rotates annually among the commissioners
- The four-vote requirement means the Commission frequently deadlocks 3-3 on partisan lines, which is a deliberate design feature favoring restraint in enforcement

### Jurisdiction

The FEC has jurisdiction over campaign finance in **federal elections only**:

- **President and Vice President**
- **U.S. Senate** (all 50 states plus D.C.)
- **U.S. House of Representatives** (435 districts plus delegates)

The FEC does **not** regulate:
- State or local elections (governed by state election agencies)
- Issue advocacy that does not expressly advocate for or against a federal candidate
- Lobbying (regulated by the Clerk of the House and Secretary of the Senate)
- Tax-exempt political organizations under IRC 527 (regulated by the IRS, though some also file with the FEC)

| Form | Name | Purpose | When Filed |
|------|------|---------|------------|
| **FEC Form 1** | Statement of Organization | Registers a political committee with the FEC | Within 10 days of reaching $5,000 threshold or designation |
| **FEC Form 2** | Statement of Candidacy | Declares candidacy for federal office | Within 15 days of becoming a candidate (raising/spending over $5,000) |

| **FEC Form 99** | Miscellaneous Text | Transmitting explanations, amendments, or other text | As needed |
| **FEC Form 1M** | Notification of Multicandidate Status | Notifies FEC that a PAC qualifies as multicandidate | After meeting qualifications |

## The $5,000 Registration Threshold

You become a **candidate** under federal law when you (or persons acting on your behalf) **raise or spend more than $5,000** in connection with a federal election. At that point:

1. You must file **FEC Form 2** (Statement of Candidacy) within **15 days**
2. You must designate a **principal campaign committee**
3. That committee must file **FEC Form 1** (Statement of Organization) within **10 days** of designation

### What counts toward the $5,000:
- Contributions received from others
- Personal funds spent on the campaign (loans to your campaign count)
- In-kind contributions (goods or services donated to the campaign at fair market value)
- "Testing the waters" expenditures if you later become a candidate (these are retroactively counted)

### What does NOT count:
- Purely personal activity (e.g., thinking about running, having private conversations)
- Activities that are clearly not campaign-related

**Important:** "Testing the waters" activities (polling, travel to assess viability, consulting with advisors) do not trigger candidacy by themselves. However, once you cross the $5,000 threshold or make a public announcement of candidacy, all prior testing-the-waters funds must be reported.

## Committee Types

### Authorized Committees
- **Principal Campaign Committee:** Every federal candidate must designate one. This is the candidate's official campaign committee.
- **Authorized Committee:** Any additional committee that the candidate authorizes to raise or spend funds on their behalf. A candidate can authorize multiple committees but must have exactly one principal campaign committee.

### Unauthorized Committees (Independent of Any Candidate)
- **PAC (Political Action Committee):** Raises funds from a "restricted class" (e.g., a corporation's employees, a union's members) and contributes to candidates. Subject to contribution limits both on receipts and on giving.
  - **Multicandidate PAC:** A PAC that has been registered for at least 6 months, received contributions from more than 50 people, and contributed to at least 5 federal candidates. Gets higher contribution limits from individuals ($5,000/year vs. $2,900 for non-multicandidate).
  - **Connected (SSF) PAC:** A Separate Segregated Fund established by a corporation, union, trade association, or membership organization. The sponsoring organization can pay administrative costs.
  - **Non-connected PAC:** A PAC with no sponsoring organization. Must pay its own administrative costs from contributions.

- **Super PAC (Independent Expenditure-Only Committee):** May raise **unlimited** funds from individuals, corporations, unions, and other entities. May spend **unlimited** amounts on independent expenditures (ads, mail, etc.) but:
  - **Cannot contribute directly to candidates**
  - **Cannot coordinate** with candidates or their campaigns on expenditures
  - Must report all donors and expenditures to the FEC

- **Hybrid PAC (Carey Committee):** Maintains two separate bank accounts -- one that operates like a traditional PAC (subject to limits, can contribute to candidates) and one that operates like a Super PAC (unlimited receipts, independent expenditures only).

### Party Committees
- **National Party Committee:** e.g., DNC, RNC. Subject to specific limits on receipts and contributions to candidates.
- **State Party Committee:** The state-level arm of a national party. Can make coordinated and independent expenditures for federal candidates.
- **Local Party Committee:** County or city party committee. Often treated as part of the state party for FEC purposes unless it independently registers.
- **National Congressional Campaign Committees:** e.g., DCCC, NRCC, DSCC, NRSC. Function as national party committees for limit purposes.

## Practical Steps to Register as a Federal Candidate

### Step-by-Step Registration Process

**Step 1: Determine if you are a candidate.**
- Have you raised or spent more than $5,000 in connection with your campaign? If yes, you are legally a candidate.
- Have you publicly announced your candidacy? Combined with any fundraising or spending, this may trigger candidacy.

**Step 2: File FEC Form 2 -- Statement of Candidacy.**
- File within **15 days** of becoming a candidate
- Include: your name, address, party affiliation, office sought, state and district
- Designate your **principal campaign committee** by name and FEC ID (if already registered)
- File electronically through the FEC's e-filing system at [fec.gov/help-candidates-and-committees/filing-reports/e-filing/](https://www.fec.gov/help-candidates-and-committees/filing-reports/e-filing/)

**Step 3: Establish your principal campaign committee.**
- Choose a committee name (must include the candidate's last name)
- Appoint a **treasurer** (required -- the committee cannot operate without one)
- Open a **campaign bank account** at a federally insured institution
- Obtain an **EIN (Employer Identification Number)** from the IRS for the committee

**Step 4: File FEC Form 1 -- Statement of Organization.**
- File within **10 days** of the committee being designated (or established)
- Include: committee name, address, treasurer name, bank/depository, candidate name and office sought, affiliated committees (if any)
- File electronically

**Step 5: Set up compliance infrastructure.**
- Choose reporting software or a compliance vendor
- Establish record-keeping procedures for all contributions and expenditures
- Train your treasurer on FEC reporting requirements
- Set up a system to collect required donor information (name, address, occupation, employer) for all contributions
- Understand your reporting schedule (quarterly for House; monthly or semi-annual option for Senate)

**Step 6: Begin operations.**
- All funds must flow through the campaign committee's bank account
- Maintain records from day one -- retroactive record-keeping is painful and error-prone
- Any "testing the waters" funds previously raised must be disclosed on the first report

## Advisory Opinions Process

An **Advisory Opinion (AO)** is a formal FEC response to a specific question about how campaign finance law applies to a particular factual situation. AOs are binding on the requestor and can be relied on by similarly situated parties.

### Who can request an AO?
- Any person or entity directly involved in the activity in question
- Candidates, committees, corporations, unions, individuals, or other political actors

### How the process works:
1. **Submit a written request** describing the specific activity and legal question
2. The FEC's **Office of General Counsel** drafts a proposed opinion
3. The draft is placed on the **public record** and open for comment (usually 10 days)
4. Commissioners vote -- **four votes** required to approve
5. The FEC must issue the opinion within **60 days** of receiving a complete request (20 days during the 60-day pre-election period)
6. If the Commission deadlocks, no opinion is issued, and the requestor does not receive guidance

### Why AOs matter for campaigns:
- They provide **safe harbor** -- if you follow an AO issued to you, you cannot be penalized
- They establish **precedent** that other similarly situated actors can rely on
- They are the primary mechanism by which the FEC addresses new technologies, fundraising methods, and novel campaign activities (e.g., cryptocurrency donations, text-to-donate, use of AI in campaigns)

### Recent AO areas of significance:
- Internet and digital advertising disclaimers
- Cryptocurrency contributions (treated as in-kind, subject to limits, must collect donor info)
- Text message fundraising platforms
- Use of campaign funds for candidate security
- Volunteer internet activity exemptions

## FEC Enforcement

The FEC enforces campaign finance law through several mechanisms:

- **Complaints (MURs -- Matters Under Review):** Anyone can file a complaint alleging a violation. Requires four votes to proceed to investigation.
- **Audits:** The FEC can audit committees that fail to meet reporting thresholds or show discrepancies.
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR):** A less adversarial process for resolving certain violations.
- **Administrative Fines Program:** Automatic penalties for late or missing reports (no Commissioner vote needed).
- **Referrals to DOJ:** For criminal violations, the FEC can refer matters to the Department of Justice.

### Common violations and their consequences:
- **Late filing:** Administrative fines calculated by formula (days late x committee size)
- **Excessive contributions:** Must refund or redesignate within specific timeframes
- **Failure to disclose:** Civil penalties, potential referral
- **Coordination with Super PACs:** Can result in treating independent expenditures as in-kind contributions (subject to limits), plus civil/criminal penalties
- **Foreign national contributions:** Criminal violation -- both the donor and the knowing recipient can face penalties
