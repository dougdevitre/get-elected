# Campaign Lifecycle Phases

This reference covers the 7 phases of a typical US political campaign from initial exploration through post-election obligations. Phases may overlap, especially Fundraising which runs continuously once begun.

---

## Phase 1: Exploring

Assessing whether a campaign is viable before making a public commitment. This involves personal reflection, conversations with family and trusted advisors, and research into the political landscape. Most potential candidates spend weeks or months here before deciding.

**Typical Timeline:** 6-18 months before filing deadline
**Key Activities:**
- Assess personal readiness and family impact
- Research the district (demographics, voting history, partisan lean)
- Identify the office and understand its responsibilities
- Talk to current/former officeholders for insight
- Evaluate potential opponents and their strengths
- Gauge initial support from community leaders
- Review personal finances and employment implications
- Consult an attorney on eligibility and conflicts of interest

**Common Mistakes:**
- Skipping family buy-in, leading to mid-campaign crises
- Underestimating time commitment (20-60 hours/week depending on office)
- Ignoring residency or eligibility requirements
- Announcing publicly before doing adequate research

**Skill Commands:** `/explore-viability`, `/district-research`, `/office-lookup`

---

## Phase 2: Filing

Completing the legal steps to become a candidate. This includes ballot access requirements, forming a campaign committee, appointing a treasurer, and opening a dedicated bank account. Requirements vary significantly by state and office level.

**Typical Timeline:** Filing deadline varies; typically 3-12 months before election day
**Key Activities:**
- Obtain and file candidacy paperwork with appropriate election authority
- Collect petition signatures if required
- Form an authorized campaign committee
- Appoint a treasurer (required for federal; varies for state/local)
- Open a dedicated campaign bank account
- Obtain an EIN from the IRS for the committee
- File a Statement of Candidacy (federal) or state equivalent
- Pay any filing fees

**Common Mistakes:**
- Missing the filing deadline (no extensions, no exceptions)
- Insufficient or invalid petition signatures
- Commingling personal and campaign funds before the bank account is open
- Failing to register the committee before accepting contributions

**Skill Commands:** `/filing-checklist`, `/ballot-access`, `/agency-lookup`

---

## Phase 3: Building

Assembling the infrastructure for a functioning campaign. This means recruiting key staff or volunteers, building a public presence, and creating a written campaign plan with goals, budget, and timeline.

**Typical Timeline:** Immediately after filing through first 1-2 months of active campaign
**Key Activities:**
- Recruit campaign manager, treasurer, and core volunteers
- Build a campaign website with donation capability
- Establish social media accounts (platform-appropriate)
- Write a campaign plan with vote goal, budget, and timeline
- Develop initial messaging and candidate bio
- Set up voter database or acquire voter file access
- Secure a campaign headquarters or meeting space
- Establish communication channels (email list, texting platform)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Launching voter contact before messaging is ready
- Overinvesting in office space vs. voter contact tools
- No written campaign plan or budget
- Neglecting to set a clear win number and vote goal

**Skill Commands:** `/campaign-plan`, `/win-number`, `/budget-template`

---

## Phase 4: Fundraising

Raising the money needed to execute the campaign plan. This is continuous once it begins and typically the most time-intensive activity for candidates. It includes direct donor solicitation, events, and online fundraising.

**Typical Timeline:** Begins at filing, continues through election day
**Key Activities:**
- Build a call list of prospective donors
- Schedule daily call time (1-3 hours for competitive races)
- Plan and execute fundraising events
- Set up online donation platform with proper disclaimers
- Track contributions and ensure compliance with limits
- Send thank-you notes within 48 hours
- File required financial disclosure reports on time
- Monitor burn rate and adjust spending

**Common Mistakes:**
- Avoiding call time or delegating it entirely
- Accepting contributions that exceed legal limits
- Missing required disclaimers on solicitation materials
- Failing to track in-kind contributions

**Skill Commands:** `/fundraising-plan`, `/contribution-limits`, `/call-time-tips`

---

## Phase 5: Running

Active campaigning: delivering the message, making direct voter contact, engaging media, and preparing for GOTV operations. This is the most visible phase and where the campaign plan meets reality.

**Typical Timeline:** 3-6 months before election day through election day
**Key Activities:**
- Execute door-to-door canvassing plan
- Run phone banking and texting programs
- Earn media coverage (press releases, events, interviews)
- Place paid advertising (mail, digital, radio, TV as budget allows)
- Prepare for and participate in debates/forums
- Build and train GOTV volunteer teams
- Track voter contact data and adjust targeting
- Manage rapid response for attacks or breaking news

**Common Mistakes:**
- Spending too much on yard signs, too little on direct voter contact
- Ignoring data and targeting (knocking random doors)
- Starting GOTV planning too late (should begin weeks before election)
- Candidate burnout from poor scheduling

**Skill Commands:** `/gotv-plan`, `/messaging-framework`, `/canvass-script`, `/media-pitch`

---

## Phase 6: Reporting

Meeting campaign finance disclosure obligations. These reports are legally required, publicly available, and carry penalties for late or inaccurate filing. Reporting begins early and continues after the election.

**Typical Timeline:** First report due shortly after committee formation; continues post-election
**Key Activities:**
- File pre-election financial reports by deadline
- File post-election financial reports
- Reconcile bank statements against reporting software
- Itemize contributions above the threshold
- Report all expenditures accurately
- Ensure employer/occupation data is collected for itemized donors
- Amend prior reports if errors are discovered
- Retain records for required period (federal: 3 years)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Missing a filing deadline (triggers automatic fines at federal level)
- Inaccurate categorization of expenditures
- Failing to itemize contributions that meet the threshold
- Not collecting employer/occupation from donors at time of contribution

**Skill Commands:** `/filing-deadlines`, `/reporting-checklist`, `/compliance-review`

---

## Phase 7: Post-Election

Win or lose, there are legal and practical obligations after election day. Winners transition to office. Losers wind down the campaign. Both must handle remaining debt, file final reports, and properly close or maintain the committee.

**Typical Timeline:** Election day through 3-12 months after (or longer if debt remains)
**Key Activities:**
- File final post-election financial reports
- Pay outstanding debts and obligations
- Send thank-you communications to donors, volunteers, staff
- If elected: begin transition planning and orientation
- If not elected: return any unused rental equipment, close accounts
- Decide whether to terminate or maintain the committee
- Handle surplus funds per applicable law (donate to charity, party, future campaign)
- Conduct a campaign debrief with key team members

**Common Mistakes:**
- Abandoning compliance obligations after a loss
- Improperly disposing of surplus campaign funds
- Failing to file termination report when closing the committee
- Neglecting to preserve records for the required retention period

**Skill Commands:** `/wind-down-checklist`, `/surplus-funds`, `/termination-report`
