# Candidate Performance: Coaching, Media Training, and Wellness

A great platform with poor delivery loses. A mediocre platform with excellent delivery wins more often than it should. This module covers the physical, vocal, and emotional skills that make a candidate effective, plus media training and the wellness practices that sustain performance over a grueling campaign.

---

## Delivery Fundamentals

### Voice

**Volume:** Project to the back of the room, not the front row. Most candidates speak too quietly. Record yourself and play it back — if you sound loud to yourself, you are probably at the right volume for the room.

**Pace:** The ideal speaking pace for political communication is 140-160 words per minute. Most nervous candidates rush to 180+. Slow down. Pauses are powerful, not awkward.

**Variation:** Monotone kills attention. Vary your pace, volume, and pitch to emphasize key points. Speed up slightly for energy, slow down for emphasis, drop your voice for intimacy, raise it for urgency.

**Warm-up:** Before any speaking event, do a 5-minute vocal warm-up: hum, say tongue twisters, read a paragraph aloud at varying volumes. Your voice is a muscle — cold muscles underperform.

### Pace and Pauses

**The power pause:** After making a key point, stop talking for 2-3 full seconds. Let the audience absorb it. Most candidates fill every pause with "um," "uh," or the next sentence. Resist. Silence after a strong line is the most powerful tool in public speaking.

**Rhythm:** Think in groups of three. Three-part lists, three beats before a punch line, three seconds of pause. The rule of three is deeply embedded in human cognition.

**Transitions:** Use clear transitions between sections: "Here is what I mean by that." "Let me tell you a story." "Now, here is the contrast." Transitions give the audience a mental break and signal structure.

### Body Language

**Posture:** Stand tall, shoulders back, weight evenly distributed. Do not lean on the podium — it signals weakness or fatigue. Do not lock your knees — it restricts blood flow and can cause fainting.

**Hands:** Use natural gestures to emphasize points. Default hand position: at your sides or loosely clasped in front of you. Avoid: hands in pockets, arms crossed, pointing at people, gripping the podium.

**Movement:** If there is no podium, move with purpose. Take 2-3 steps toward the audience when making a personal point. Plant your feet when making a strong statement. Do not pace randomly — it signals anxiety.

**Facial expression:** Match your expression to your message. A serious point with a smile is confusing. A positive point with a furrowed brow is off-putting. Practice in front of a mirror or record yourself.

### Eye Contact

**One-on-one:** Maintain eye contact 60-70% of the time. Look at the person when you are making a point. Break eye contact naturally when thinking or transitioning.

**Small group:** Divide the room into zones. Spend 3-5 seconds making eye contact with someone in each zone, then move to the next. Do not let your eyes dart around — it looks nervous.

**Large group:** Look at sections of the audience, not individuals. Sweep your gaze slowly from left to center to right. Look at the balcony or back rows occasionally — they feel ignored.

**Camera:** Look directly into the lens, not at the monitor or the interviewer off-camera. The lens is the voter's eyes. Practice by recording selfie videos and talking directly into the phone camera.

### Managing Nerves

**Reframe anxiety as energy.** The physiological symptoms of anxiety (elevated heart rate, adrenaline, heightened alertness) are identical to excitement. Tell yourself "I am excited" instead of "I am nervous." Research shows this simple reframing improves performance.

**Breathe:** Before stepping on stage or into an interview, take 5 deep breaths: 4 counts in, 4 counts hold, 6 counts out. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and calms the fight-or-flight response.

**Prepare obsessively, then let go.** Anxiety comes from uncertainty. If you have prepared your remarks, practiced your answers, and done your homework, trust yourself. The preparation is in you.

**Have an anchor phrase.** A single sentence you know cold that you can always return to if you lose your place: your core campaign message. If your mind goes blank, say your anchor phrase. It buys you time and keeps you on message.

### Handling Hostile Questions

**Rule 1: Never take the bait.** A hostile question is designed to provoke an emotional reaction. The moment you get angry, defensive, or flustered, the questioner wins.

**Rule 2: Acknowledge, then redirect.**
- "I appreciate the question. Here is how I see it..."
- "I understand the concern. Let me address that directly..."
- "That is an important point, and here is where I stand..."

**Rule 3: Answer the question you wish they had asked.** This is called "bridging." You acknowledge their question, provide a brief response, then pivot to your strongest ground. "The real issue here is..." or "What voters are telling me is..."

**Rule 4: Stay physically calm.** Do not lean forward aggressively, raise your voice, or point. Keep your posture open and your voice steady. Physical calm signals confidence.

**Rule 5: Know when to stop.** Answer once, clearly and confidently. If the questioner follows up aggressively, you can say: "I've given you my answer. I want to make sure everyone has a chance to ask their question."

### The Pivot

The pivot is the most important debate and interview skill. Structure:

1. **Acknowledge:** Show you heard the question (1 sentence)
2. **Bridge:** Transition to your message ("But the bigger issue is..." / "What I hear from voters is..." / "Let me put that in context...")
3. **Deliver:** State your message clearly (2-3 sentences)

Practice pivots until they are automatic. You should be able to pivot from any question to your core message in under 15 seconds.

## Media Training

### Television

**Do's:**
- Look at the interviewer during the interview, not the camera (unless told otherwise by the producer)
- Sit up straight, lean slightly forward — it projects engagement
- Use short, declarative sentences. TV quotes are 8-12 seconds. If your answer is longer than 20 seconds, you are giving them the edit.
- Wear solid colors. Avoid busy patterns, small stripes, or shiny fabrics.
- Arrive early. Walk the set. Know where the cameras are.

**Don'ts:**
- Do not say "no comment" — it sounds guilty. Say "Here is what I can tell you."
- Do not repeat a negative framing. If asked "Are you corrupt?" do not say "I am not corrupt." Say "My record of service speaks for itself."
- Do not look at the camera unless you are delivering a direct-to-viewer statement.
- Do not fidget, swivel in your chair, or touch your face.

### Radio

**Do's:**
- Smile while you talk. It changes your vocal quality and makes you sound warmer.
- Use vivid language — radio is a visual medium powered by words.
- Have your talking points printed in front of you (radio audience cannot see them).
- Stand up while doing phone interviews if possible — it improves vocal energy.

**Don'ts:**
- Do not rustle papers near the microphone.
- Do not use filler words — "um" and "uh" are more noticeable on radio.
- Do not give one-word answers. Radio needs you to fill space.

### Print / Online Reporters

**Do's:**
- Assume everything is on the record unless you explicitly negotiate otherwise before speaking.
- Provide quotable sound bites — journalists want sentences they can use verbatim.
- Offer to follow up with additional information or sources.
- Take your time. Print interviews do not have the time pressure of broadcast.

**Don'ts:**
- Do not say things you would not want in a headline.
- Do not speculate or answer hypotheticals.
- Do not say "off the record" after saying something — it does not work retroactively.

### Soundbite Construction

A good soundbite is:
- **Short:** 8-15 seconds spoken (15-30 words written)
- **Self-contained:** Makes sense without context
- **Quotable:** Uses vivid, active language
- **Memorable:** Has rhythm, contrast, or imagery

**Formula:** "[Specific fact or observation] — and that is why [clear position or call to action]."

**Example:** "Property taxes have gone up 23% while services have gone down. That is why I am calling for a full spending audit on Day 1."

## Candidate Wellness

### Burnout Warning Signs

- Irritability and short temper with staff, family, or voters
- Declining performance at events (less energy, less engagement)
- Difficulty sleeping despite exhaustion
- Loss of appetite or overeating
- Withdrawal from people (canceling personal commitments, isolating)
- Cynicism about the campaign or voters
- Physical symptoms: frequent illness, headaches, back pain
- Inability to concentrate or make decisions

**If you see three or more of these signs, the candidate needs intervention: a day off, a conversation, or a schedule adjustment.**

### Physical Health

**Sleep:** Minimum 6 hours per night, target 7. Nothing degrades performance faster than sleep deprivation. Campaign managers: schedule late-night events sparingly.

**Nutrition:** Eat real meals, not just campaign trail snacks. Pack healthy food for the road. Stay hydrated — dehydration causes fatigue and brain fog.

**Exercise:** 30 minutes of physical activity at least 4 days per week. Walking, running, gym, yoga — whatever the candidate will actually do. Schedule it like a meeting so it does not get bumped.

**Medical care:** Keep all regular medical appointments. Get a checkup before the campaign intensifies. Carry any prescription medications at all times.

### Mental Health

**Normalize it.** Campaigning is psychologically demanding: constant judgment, public scrutiny, rejection at doors, attacks from opponents, uncertain outcome. It is normal to struggle.

**Support system:** Identify 2-3 people outside the campaign the candidate can talk to honestly: a spouse, a close friend, a therapist, a mentor. These people provide perspective that campaign staff cannot.

**Decompression rituals:** The candidate needs a daily practice that separates campaign time from personal time. This could be exercise, meditation, reading, cooking, prayer, or just 15 minutes of silence. It must be daily and non-negotiable.

**Professional help:** If the candidate shows signs of depression, anxiety disorder, or substance use escalation, the campaign manager should privately and compassionately encourage professional help. This is not weakness — it is responsible self-management.

## Family Resilience

### Preparing the Family

Before the campaign launches, the candidate should have an honest conversation with their family:

**Topics to cover:**
- Time commitment: how much the candidate will be away, schedule impact
- Public scrutiny: what may be said about the family, social media comments, news coverage
- Financial impact: campaign costs, income disruption, fundraising obligations
- Emotional toll: stress, frustration, uncertainty, impact on relationships
- Roles: what the family is willing to do publicly (events, photos, statements) and what is off-limits
- Exit conditions: under what circumstances would the candidate withdraw or pause

### Protecting Children

- Children should never be forced to participate in campaign activities
- Establish clear rules about children's appearance in campaign materials and social media (parental consent for every use)
- Monitor social media for any mentions of children and respond swiftly to inappropriate content
- Brief children's schools and caregivers about the campaign so they are prepared for attention
- Maintain children's routines as much as possible — consistency is their anchor during disruption

### The Family Conversation (Ongoing)

Schedule a weekly family check-in (not about campaign strategy — about how everyone is doing). Ask:
- "How are you feeling about the campaign this week?"
- "Is there anything that bothered or worried you?"
- "What do you need from me that you are not getting?"
- "Is there anything coming up that we should talk about?"

This conversation should happen every week without exception. Cancel a campaign event before canceling the family check-in.

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Mirror Drill (5 minutes daily)
Stand in front of a mirror and deliver your 30-second stump speech. Watch your facial expressions, hand gestures, and posture. Adjust until your visual delivery matches your message.

### Exercise 2: The Timer Drill (10 minutes, 3x/week)
Set a timer for 30 seconds. Answer a random issue question within the time limit. Practice until you can consistently deliver a complete door response (acknowledge, position, personal, bridge) in 30 seconds.

### Exercise 3: The Hostile Question Drill (15 minutes, 2x/week)
Have a staff member or friend ask you the hardest, most unfair questions they can think of. Practice acknowledging, bridging, and delivering without getting flustered. Record yourself and review.

### Exercise 4: The Camera Drill (10 minutes, 2x/week)
Record yourself answering three questions on your phone camera. Watch the playback. Look for: eye contact with the lens, filler words, fidgeting, vocal monotone, answer length. Improve each session.

### Exercise 5: The Soundbite Challenge (10 minutes, weekly)
Take three campaign topics. For each, write and practice a soundbite under 15 seconds that is quotable, self-contained, and memorable. Test them on a friend — can they repeat it back to you?
