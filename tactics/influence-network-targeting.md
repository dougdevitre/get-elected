# Influence Network Targeting: The Power Map Module

Every community has a hidden power structure — the people whose opinions, relationships, and decisions shape how others think and act. A Power Map identifies these individuals, scores their importance, and sequences outreach to systematically build the candidate's influence network.

---

## Why Power Mapping Wins Races

Voters do not make decisions in isolation. They are influenced by people they trust: their pastor, their boss, their neighbor who "knows about politics," the business owner they see every day. Winning these influencers wins their networks.

A campaign that converts 50 key influencers can move 5,000 votes through downstream effects. A campaign that ignores power mapping spends all its time on retail voter contact and misses the wholesale opportunity.

---

## The 12 Categories of Influential People

### 1. Elected Officials

**Who:** Current officeholders at every level — city council, county board, state legislature, Congress, constitutional officers, judges (where endorsements are permitted).
**Why they matter:** Institutional credibility, fundraising networks, organizational infrastructure, media access.
**Key question:** Will they endorse, stay neutral, or support the opponent?

### 2. Party Leaders

**Who:** County party chairs, state committee members, precinct captains, national committee members, party caucus leaders.
**Why they matter:** Control party resources, endorsement processes, voter file access, volunteer pipelines.
**Key question:** What do they need to feel comfortable supporting you?

### 3. Major Donors

**Who:** Individuals who give $1,000+ regularly to political campaigns, bundlers, PAC directors, family foundations active in politics.
**Why they matter:** Fund campaigns directly, signal viability to other donors, often connected to business and social networks.
**Key question:** Who do they usually support, and what would change their pattern?

### 4. Business Leaders

**Who:** CEOs, presidents, and owners of the largest employers and most visible businesses in the district. Chamber of Commerce leadership. Major developers and commercial property owners.
**Why they matter:** Economic credibility, employee influence, fundraising capacity, community visibility.
**Key question:** What business-related issues will determine their support?

### 5. Faith Leaders

**Who:** Senior pastors of large congregations, rabbis, imams, priests, denominational leaders, chaplains.
**Why they matter:** Moral authority, congregation access, trusted voice on values-based issues.
**Key question:** What community concerns are they hearing from their congregation?

### 6. Education Leaders

**Who:** School board members, superintendents, principals, teachers union presidents, college presidents, PTA council leaders, prominent professors.
**Why they matter:** Touch every family with school-age children, respected community voices, organized networks.
**Key question:** What education issues are top-of-mind, and where does the candidate align?

### 7. Nonprofit Leaders

**Who:** Executive directors of major local nonprofits, foundation leaders, United Way directors, community development corporation leaders.
**Why they matter:** Deep community relationships, issue expertise, networks of engaged citizens.
**Key question:** What policy changes would most benefit their mission?

### 8. Media Figures

**Who:** Local newspaper editors and reporters, TV news anchors, radio hosts, podcast hosts, newsletter writers, bloggers with significant local followings.
**Why they matter:** Control the information environment, shape public narrative, amplify or bury stories.
**Key question:** What stories are they looking for, and how can the campaign provide them?

### 9. Union Leaders

**Who:** Local presidents, business agents, regional directors, labor council officers, shop stewards at major employers.
**Why they matter:** Organized member mobilization, endorsement infrastructure, fundraising, political sophistication.
**Key question:** What is their endorsement process, and which issues are non-negotiable?

### 10. Community Organizers

**Who:** Leaders of neighborhood associations, tenant organizations, civic groups, advocacy organizations, community land trusts, mutual aid networks.
**Why they matter:** Mobilize grassroots energy, deep relationships in specific communities, trusted by people who distrust institutions.
**Key question:** What are their priorities, and how can the campaign support their work?

### 11. Cultural Influencers

**Who:** Local artists, musicians, athletes, restaurant owners, event promoters, cultural institution directors, community historians.
**Why they matter:** Cultural credibility, access to audiences that traditional politics does not reach, social media presence.
**Key question:** What would make them excited about this campaign?

### 12. Social Media Influencers

**Who:** Local accounts with 5,000+ followers, community Facebook group administrators, popular local TikTok creators, Nextdoor power users, local Twitter/X commentators.
**Why they matter:** Amplification, peer influence in digital spaces, trend-setting.
**Key question:** What content would they share, and what would make them an advocate?

## Scoring Matrix

Rate each individual on five dimensions (1-5 scale):

| Dimension | 1 (Low) | 3 (Medium) | 5 (High) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reach** | Influences < 50 people | Influences 50-500 | Influences 500+ |
| **Relevance** | Tangential to target voters | Moderate overlap | Direct access to key voter segments |
| **Credibility** | Limited trust/authority | Respected in their circle | Widely trusted across communities |
| **Accessibility** | No connection, hostile | Second-degree connection, neutral | Direct connection, favorable |
| **Activation potential** | Unlikely to do more than endorse | Would attend events, make calls | Would actively campaign, fundraise, deploy network |

**Total score (5-25):**
- 20-25: Tier 1 priority. Candidate personal outreach within 2 weeks.
- 15-19: Tier 2 priority. Campaign manager outreach within 30 days.
- 10-14: Tier 3 priority. Staff or volunteer outreach within 60 days.
- 5-9: Monitor. Maintain awareness, engage if opportunity arises.

## Outreach Sequence

### Phase 1: Research (Weeks 1-2)

1. Brainstorm every influential person in the district across all 12 categories
2. Score each person using the matrix above
3. Map relationships: who knows whom, who influences whom, who are rivals
4. Identify the candidate's existing connections to each person
5. Research their public positions, interests, and recent activities

### Phase 2: Warm-Up (Weeks 3-4)

1. Engage on social media: follow, like, comment on their posts (genuinely, not robotically)
2. Attend their events: show up at their organization's functions, their business openings, their community programs
3. Send a personal note or email: "I admire your work on [specific thing]. I'd love to connect."
4. Have mutual connections make introductions where possible

### Phase 3: The Meeting (Weeks 5-8)

1. Request a one-on-one meeting (coffee, their office, their preferred venue)
2. Listen first: ask about their priorities, concerns, community observations
3. Share your campaign's vision and how it aligns with their interests
4. Make a specific ask calibrated to their level of engagement
5. Follow up within 48 hours with a thank-you and next steps

### Phase 4: Activation (Ongoing)

1. Deliver on any commitments you made during the meeting
2. Keep them informed with periodic updates (monthly at most)
3. Invite them to participate: endorsement, event appearance, introduction to their network
4. Recognize their support publicly when they are ready
5. Continue relationship-building regardless of whether they formally endorse

## Package Templates

### Influencer Briefing Package (for Tier 1 targets)

Contents:
- Personal letter from the candidate (handwritten greeting, typed body)
- Campaign one-pager: candidate bio, key priorities, path to victory
- Issue brief relevant to their area of influence
- List of endorsements received to date
- Recent press coverage (2-3 favorable clips)
- Specific ask and proposed next step

### Quick Introduction Package (for Tier 2-3 targets)

Contents:
- Campaign palm card or brochure
- Brief personal note from the candidate or campaign manager
- Link to campaign website and social media
- Contact information for follow-up

## Power Map Database Schema

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Name | Full name |
| Category | Which of the 12 categories (may be multiple) |
| Title/Role | Current position |
| Organization | Their primary affiliation |
| Reach Score | 1-5 |
| Relevance Score | 1-5 |
| Credibility Score | 1-5 |
| Accessibility Score | 1-5 |
| Activation Score | 1-5 |
| Total Score | Sum of above |
| Tier | 1, 2, 3, or Monitor |
| Connection | How the campaign knows them (or path to introduction) |
| Key Issues | What they care about most |
| Political History | Past endorsements, donations, party affiliation |
| Status | Not contacted / Warm-up / Meeting scheduled / Met / Activated / Declined |
| Outreach Owner | Staff member responsible |
| Last Contact Date | Most recent interaction |
| Next Step | Specific planned action and deadline |
| Notes | Context, relationship dynamics, concerns |

## /powermap Command Workflow

When a user invokes the /powermap command, follow this sequence:

1. **Gather context:** Ask for the district/jurisdiction, office sought, and candidate's existing network of relationships.
2. **Generate categories:** Produce a starter list of influential people across all 12 categories using the district's characteristics (urban/rural/suburban, major employers, cultural institutions, political dynamics).
3. **Score and prioritize:** Apply the scoring matrix to rank all individuals.
4. **Sequence outreach:** Generate a 60-day outreach calendar with specific actions for each tier.
5. **Create packages:** Draft personalized briefing materials for Tier 1 targets.
6. **Track progress:** Provide a tracking template pre-populated with the identified influencers.

## Relationship Mapping Rules

1. **Never burn a bridge.** A "no" today may become a "yes" after a primary, an endorsement shift, or a policy evolution.
2. **Respect the chain.** Some influencers must be approached through intermediaries. Going direct when protocol requires an introduction is a mistake.
3. **Reciprocity matters.** Before asking for something, give something: amplify their cause, attend their event, connect them with a useful contact.
4. **Confidentiality is currency.** Never share one influencer's private position with another. Trust, once broken, is never rebuilt.
5. **Update the map monthly.** Power structures shift. People gain and lose influence. New players emerge. The map is a living document.

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Only mapping people you already know.** The most valuable targets are often people outside the candidate's existing network.
2. **Ignoring Tier 3 influencers.** A barber who talks to 30 people a day may move more votes than an elected official who ignores your race.
3. **Treating the power map as a task list instead of a relationship strategy.** The goal is genuine connection, not transactional box-checking.
4. **Failing to map influence relationships between targets.** If Person A influences Person B, winning Person A first makes Person B easier.
5. **Not updating scores as the campaign progresses.** Accessibility changes as you build momentum. Relevance changes as issues shift. Re-score quarterly.
