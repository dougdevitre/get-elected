# Voter Personas: Psychographic Voter Profiling

Voter personas translate abstract data into concrete people the campaign can message to, organize around, and design programs for. Instead of targeting "women 35-54," you target "Maria, the working mom who drops her kids at school, commutes 40 minutes, worries about childcare costs, and makes voting decisions based on who she trusts to understand her life."

---

## Why Personas Work

1. **Specificity sharpens messaging.** Writing to "a persona" produces better copy than writing to "voters."
2. **Channel selection becomes obvious.** You know where Maria is (school pickup, Facebook, neighborhood app). You know where a generic "suburban woman" is? Everywhere and nowhere.
3. **Volunteers connect better.** A canvasser who has internalized "you might meet a Maria at this door" performs better than one armed only with a script.
4. **Resource allocation improves.** When you know which personas are most persuadable and where to reach them, you stop wasting resources on the wrong channels.

---

## The Persona Framework

Each persona includes seven components:

### 1. Demographics
Age range, gender, race/ethnicity, income bracket, education level, household composition, housing type (own/rent, house/apartment), geography within the district.

### 2. Psychographics
Values, worldview, political identity (strong partisan, soft partisan, independent, disengaged), information sources, community involvement, trust level in institutions, risk tolerance, change orientation (wants change vs. wants stability).

### 3. Daily Life
What does a typical day look like? Where do they go, what do they see, who do they talk to? This determines when and where the campaign can reach them.

### 4. Issue Priorities
Top 3 issues driving their vote. Not what you think they should care about — what they actually care about, discovered through canvassing data, polling, and community listening.

### 5. Decision Triggers
What moves them from undecided to decided? A trusted endorsement? A specific policy? Meeting the candidate? A contrast with the opponent? Fear of the alternative? A sense of momentum?

### 6. Barriers to Support
What might stop them from voting for your candidate? Partisan identity, a specific issue disagreement, distrust of politicians, lack of information, skepticism that voting matters, logistical barriers (registration, transportation, work schedule).

### 7. Recommended Approach
- **Message:** The one thing to say that resonates with this persona
- **Channel:** Where to reach them (door, mail, text, social platform, event, radio, etc.)
- **Messenger:** Who is most credible (candidate, peer, community leader, endorser)
- **Ask:** What to ask them to do (vote, volunteer, donate, attend, share)

## Sample Personas

### Persona 1: The Concerned Parent

**Demographics:** 32-48, any gender, household income $50K-$90K, has school-age children, homeowner or long-term renter, lives in suburban or family-oriented urban neighborhood.

**Psychographics:** Pragmatic, not strongly partisan, votes for "the person not the party," gets information from local Facebook groups and other parents, moderately involved in school and community activities, trusts teachers and pediatricians more than politicians.

**Daily life:** School drop-off, work (often hybrid or flexible), grocery run, kids' activities, family dinner, collapses in front of screens after kids go to bed. Weekends are sports games and errands.

**Issue priorities:** (1) School quality and funding, (2) childcare costs, (3) neighborhood safety.

**Decision triggers:** A specific education plan they find credible. Meeting the candidate at a school event. Hearing from another parent they trust that this candidate is solid.

**Barriers:** "All politicians are the same." Too busy to research candidates. May not know an election is happening for local offices.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "[Candidate] is a parent who gets it — a real plan for our schools, not just promises."
- Channel: Facebook groups, school event appearances, targeted digital ads, lit drop at school pickup zones
- Messenger: Fellow parent who is a campaign supporter, PTA leader, popular teacher
- Ask: Come to a "Parents for [Candidate]" coffee, then vote and tell two other parents

---

### Persona 2: The Retired Veteran

**Demographics:** 62-78, predominantly male (but do not exclude women veterans), fixed or moderate income, homeowner, often lives in established neighborhood or small town, may be widowed or living alone.

**Psychographics:** Strong sense of duty and service, patriotic, values order and respect, skeptical of government waste but believes in government's role, information from local newspaper, TV news, VFW/Legion post conversations, may listen to talk radio.

**Daily life:** Morning coffee and newspaper, errands, lunch at the diner or VFW, afternoon at home or volunteering, evening news. Regular weekly schedule: church, VFW meeting, maybe a part-time job or volunteer commitment.

**Issue priorities:** (1) VA healthcare and veterans services, (2) public safety and law enforcement support, (3) property taxes and government spending.

**Decision triggers:** Candidate's military service (if applicable), endorsement from a veteran they respect, a visit to the VFW post, demonstrated commitment to fiscal responsibility.

**Barriers:** Partisan loyalty (may always vote one party), skepticism of younger candidates, perception that the candidate does not understand military culture.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "[Candidate] believes in service — service to country, service to community, and service to the people who served us."
- Channel: VFW/Legion post appearances, local newspaper, direct mail, phone call from a fellow veteran
- Messenger: Respected local veteran, retired officer, Legion post commander
- Ask: Vote, and bring a friend from the post to vote with you

---

### Persona 3: The Young Professional

**Demographics:** 24-35, college-educated, single or in a relationship without children, renting an apartment, income $35K-$75K, urban or inner-ring suburban.

**Psychographics:** Idealistic but increasingly pragmatic, information from social media (Instagram, TikTok, Reddit, podcasts), values authenticity and transparency, skeptical of traditional institutions, environmentally conscious, motivated by fairness and opportunity.

**Daily life:** Commute or remote work, lunch with coworkers, gym or social activity after work, dinner out or cooking, social media scrolling, weekend brunches, concerts, or outdoor activities.

**Issue priorities:** (1) Housing affordability and rent costs, (2) student debt and economic opportunity, (3) climate and environment.

**Decision triggers:** A candidate who feels genuine and accessible. Seeing the candidate on their social media feeds. A peer sharing a post or endorsement. An issue stance that directly affects their wallet.

**Barriers:** Cynicism about politics, may not be registered at current address, may not know about the election, feels their vote does not matter in local races.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "[Candidate] is fighting for a community where you can actually afford to build a life."
- Channel: Instagram, TikTok, targeted digital ads, events at breweries/coffee shops, apartment complex canvassing
- Messenger: Peer their age, local business owner they patronize, social media influencer
- Ask: Follow the campaign, register to vote (include link), show up at a "Politics and Pints" event

---

### Persona 4: The Small Business Owner

**Demographics:** 38-60, any gender, owns a local business (retail, restaurant, service, professional), income varies widely, deep community roots, works long hours.

**Psychographics:** Independent-minded, pragmatic, frustrated with regulation and taxes, values community investment, information from Chamber of Commerce, local business networks, and industry publications. May be politically moderate, distrustful of both parties.

**Daily life:** At the business by 7 AM, managing employees and customers all day, dealing with vendors, bills, and government paperwork, home late, little free time.

**Issue priorities:** (1) Taxes and regulation, (2) local economic development, (3) workforce availability and quality.

**Decision triggers:** A candidate who visits their business and listens, a specific policy that reduces their burden, endorsement from a business leader they respect.

**Barriers:** Too busy to engage in politics, skeptical that politicians understand business, may think local government is irrelevant to their challenges.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "[Candidate] understands what it takes to make payroll — and will fight to make it easier for businesses like yours to thrive."
- Channel: Business walk (candidate visits shops), Chamber events, targeted mail to business addresses, small business roundtable
- Messenger: Fellow business owner, Chamber president, SCORE mentor
- Ask: Endorsement (even informal: "Can we put a sign in your window?"), attend a roundtable, donate

---

### Persona 5: The Community Elder

**Demographics:** 65-85, any gender, long-term resident (20+ years in the same neighborhood), moderate to low income, deeply embedded in community networks (church, neighborhood association, extended family nearby).

**Psychographics:** Values tradition and community cohesion, has seen many elections and many candidates come and go, skeptical of newcomers, information from church, neighbors, and local news, decisive influence over family and friends' voting.

**Daily life:** Morning routine, church or community group, visiting with neighbors and family, gardening or light activity, regular weekly engagements (choir, card group, community meeting), early to bed.

**Issue priorities:** (1) Neighborhood preservation and safety, (2) affordable healthcare and prescription costs, (3) respect for community institutions and traditions.

**Decision triggers:** Personal relationship with the candidate (they need to have met you), endorsement from their pastor or a respected neighbor, sense that the candidate respects what the community has built.

**Barriers:** Distrust of outsiders and newcomers, loyalty to incumbents or established candidates, physical barriers to voting (mobility, transportation), may not respond to digital outreach.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "[Candidate] will protect this neighborhood and the community you built."
- Channel: Church appearances, home visits, neighborhood association meetings, direct mail, phone calls from family members
- Messenger: Their pastor, a neighbor they trust, a family member who supports the candidate
- Ask: Vote, talk to three neighbors, allow a yard sign

---

### Persona 6: The First-Time Voter

**Demographics:** 18-22, student or early career, living with family or in campus housing, low or no income, newly registered or not yet registered.

**Psychographics:** Excited or anxious about first election, forming political identity, heavily influenced by peers and social media, passionate about 1-2 issues, may feel the system is broken but still idealistic enough to try.

**Daily life:** Classes or entry-level job, socializing, social media (TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat), gaming, part-time work, living with roommates or family.

**Issue priorities:** (1) Climate and environment, (2) gun violence, (3) cost of education and future economic opportunity.

**Decision triggers:** A peer asking them to vote, a viral social media moment, feeling personally connected to the candidate's story, a registration drive that makes it easy.

**Barriers:** Does not know how to register or where to vote, may not feel knowledgeable enough, overwhelmed by choices, logistical confusion, not being contacted by any campaign.

**Recommended approach:**
- Message: "This is your community too. Your first vote is your first say in its future."
- Channel: Campus events, TikTok/Instagram, peer texting, voter registration drives, dorm canvassing
- Messenger: A peer their age, a professor or coach they respect, a young campaign staffer
- Ask: Register right now (hand them the form or link), commit to a voting buddy, attend one campus event

## Generating Personas for a Specific District

When building personas for a campaign, follow this process:

1. **Gather data:** Census demographics, voter file analysis (age, gender, vote history, party registration), canvassing data (issues mentioned at doors), polling (if available), community listening.
2. **Identify clusters:** Look for 4-6 distinct voter types that represent significant portions of the electorate and have distinct needs and channels.
3. **Name and humanize:** Give each persona a name, a photo (stock or representative), and a one-paragraph story. The campaign team should be able to picture this person.
4. **Validate:** Test personas against canvassing feedback. Do volunteers recognize these people at the door?
5. **Map to strategy:** For each persona, assign a message, channel, messenger, and ask. Allocate campaign resources accordingly.
6. **Update quarterly:** Personas should evolve as the campaign learns more from voter contact.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too many personas.** If you have more than 6, you cannot resource them all. Pick the ones that represent the most voters and the biggest strategic opportunity.
2. **Personas based on stereotypes, not data.** Validate with real voter contact data, not assumptions.
3. **Ignoring personas once created.** Every piece of content, every event, every mailer should be designed with a specific persona in mind.
4. **Static personas.** Update them as you learn more from voter contact. The persona you build in March may not match what you learn at doors in August.
5. **Forgetting that personas are tools, not people.** Real voters are more complex. Personas guide strategy — they do not replace genuine human connection at the door.
