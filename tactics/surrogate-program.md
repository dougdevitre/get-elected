# Surrogate Program: Building and Managing Campaign Surrogates

A surrogate program extends the candidate's reach by deploying trusted voices who speak on the candidate's behalf. Every hour a surrogate spends at an event, on a call, or on camera is an hour the candidate gains back for fundraising and high-leverage activities.

---

## The 3-Tier Surrogate Model

### Tier 1: High-Profile Surrogates

**Who they are:** Elected officials, celebrities, major endorsers, former officeholders, nationally recognized activists, prominent academics, well-known business leaders.

**What they do:** Generate media coverage, validate the candidate's credibility, headline large events, record video endorsements, participate in major fundraising events.

**Recruitment strategy:**
- Start with the candidate's existing relationships and work outward
- Have the candidate make personal asks — never delegate the initial request for Tier 1 surrogates
- Offer a clear, limited commitment (e.g., "two events and one video over three months")
- Frame participation around the surrogate's own brand and values — show them how this helps their profile too
- Provide staff support so their involvement is effortless
- For elected officials: identify shared policy priorities, past legislative partnerships, or mutual allies
- For celebrities: connect through agents or managers, lead with cause alignment, not politics

**Vetting process:**
1. Public records search (social media audit going back 10 years, news archives, court records)
2. Review of past political statements and endorsements for conflicts
3. Confirm no pending legal, financial, or personal controversies
4. Check for business interests that could create conflicts with candidate's platform
5. Verify standing in their own community — is the endorsement net-positive?
6. Internal discussion: could this surrogate become a liability? What is the worst headline?

**Deployment guidelines:**
- Major rallies and campaign launches
- Media appearances and press conferences
- High-dollar fundraising events
- Endorsement announcements (timed for maximum impact)
- Video/digital content for social media amplification
- Earned media opportunities (op-eds under their name, radio interviews)

---

### Tier 2: Community Leaders

**Who they are:** Local business owners, faith leaders, nonprofit directors, school board members, union stewards, coaches, PTA presidents, neighborhood association leaders.

**What they do:** Provide grassroots credibility, host events in their communities, make introductions to their networks, provide testimonials, serve as local media spokespeople.

**Recruitment strategy:**
- Campaign manager or field director makes the initial outreach
- Identify leaders through community mapping: who do people in this neighborhood listen to?
- Attend community events and build relationships before making the ask
- Partner with allied organizations to identify respected voices
- Frame participation as community investment: "This campaign is about your neighborhood's future"
- Offer visibility for their cause or organization in exchange for support
- Build a pipeline: every community event the candidate attends should produce 2-3 new Tier 2 prospects

**Vetting process:**
1. Google search and social media review (past 5 years)
2. Confirm standing in their community through back-channel references
3. Check for any public controversies or positions that conflict with the campaign
4. Verify their actual influence — do people follow their lead, or just know their name?
5. Quick conversation about the campaign's key positions to ensure alignment

**Deployment guidelines:**
- Community forums and town halls
- Neighborhood canvass kickoffs (they knock doors alongside volunteers)
- Local media interviews and letters to the editor
- House parties and small-group events in their networks
- Introductions to key community stakeholders
- Faith community outreach (voter registration drives, candidate forums)
- Small business roundtables

---

### Tier 3: Peer Surrogates

**Who they are:** Enthusiastic volunteers, grassroots supporters, individual donors who are passionate about the candidate, friends-and-family network members.

**What they do:** Spread the candidate's message through their personal networks using organic, peer-to-peer channels.

**Recruitment strategy:**
- Identify from volunteer database: who shows up consistently and speaks well?
- Look for supporters who are already posting about the campaign on social media
- Field organizers nominate top volunteers for the surrogate program
- Run "Ambassador" recruitment at campaign events — formalize what passionate supporters are already doing
- Make it feel like an honor: "You've been selected for our Ambassadors program"
- Keep the barrier to entry low but the standards clear

**Vetting process:**
1. Review social media presence for red flags
2. Field organizer vouch — has this person been reliable and on-message?
3. Brief conversation to confirm enthusiasm and alignment
4. No formal background check required, but flag any concerns to campaign manager

**Deployment guidelines:**
- Relational organizing: personal outreach to friends, family, coworkers
- Social media sharing and content amplification
- Letter-writing campaigns (letters to the editor, postcards to voters)
- Phone banks and text banks as message carriers, not just script readers
- Neighborhood visibility (yard signs, bumper stickers, wearing campaign gear)
- Introduction calls to their personal contacts
- Hosting informal gatherings (backyard meetups, coffee conversations)

## Surrogate Briefing Document Template

Every surrogate receives a briefing packet before any deployment. Tier 1 surrogates get a personalized version with staff review. Tier 2 and Tier 3 receive a standardized version updated monthly.

```
============================================================
SURROGATE BRIEFING DOCUMENT
[CAMPAIGN NAME] — [DATE]
CONFIDENTIAL — NOT FOR DISTRIBUTION
============================================================

SECTION 1: CANDIDATE BIO
----------------------------------------------------------
Full Name:
Office Sought:
Party:
District/Jurisdiction:

Professional Background:
[2-3 sentence summary of career and qualifications]

Personal Background:
[Family, community ties, relevant personal story]

Why They're Running:
[The candidate's core motivation in 2-3 sentences — this is
what surrogates should internalize and convey in their own words]

Key Accomplishments:
- [Accomplishment 1 — quantify if possible]
- [Accomplishment 2]
- [Accomplishment 3]
- [Accomplishment 4]

SECTION 2: KEY MESSAGES
----------------------------------------------------------
Primary Message (The Bumper Sticker):
[One sentence that captures the campaign's core argument]

Three Pillars:
1. [Issue Pillar 1]: [2 sentences + supporting fact]
2. [Issue Pillar 2]: [2 sentences + supporting fact]
3. [Issue Pillar 3]: [2 sentences + supporting fact]

The Contrast (Use Only When Asked):
"While [opponent] has [specific action/position], [candidate]
has [specific action/position]. The choice is clear."

Personal Story to Share:
[A specific anecdote about the candidate that humanizes them
and connects to the campaign message. Surrogates should tell
this in their own words, not memorize it.]

SECTION 3: DO'S AND DON'TS
----------------------------------------------------------
DO:
- Speak from personal experience — why YOU support the candidate
- Use the candidate's first name (it conveys familiarity)
- Bridge every answer back to the campaign's core message
- Share your own story of how the candidate's platform affects you
- Refer policy questions you can't answer to campaign staff
- Report any media interactions to the communications team

DON'T:
- Speak on behalf of the candidate on issues not in this brief
- Make promises about what the candidate will do in office
- Attack the opponent personally — contrast on issues only
- Engage with hostile questioners — pivot or disengage
- Freelance on social media without using approved messaging
- Discuss internal campaign strategy, polling, or fundraising
- Speculate about endorsements or future plans

SECTION 4: ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
----------------------------------------------------------
Q: Why should voters support [candidate] over [opponent]?
A: [Approved response focusing on positive vision, then contrast]

Q: What is [candidate]'s position on [hot-button issue 1]?
A: [Clear, concise position statement]

Q: What is [candidate]'s position on [hot-button issue 2]?
A: [Clear, concise position statement]

Q: What about [candidate's vulnerability/opposition attack]?
A: [Inoculation response — acknowledge, reframe, bridge to strength]

Q: Why are you personally supporting [candidate]?
A: [Surrogates should prepare their own authentic answer.
   Guidance: Lead with personal experience, connect to values,
   be specific about what convinced you.]

IF YOU DON'T KNOW THE ANSWER:
"That's a great question. I want to make sure I give you
accurate information, so let me connect you with the campaign
team. Can I get your contact information?"

SECTION 5: LOGISTICS & CONTACTS
----------------------------------------------------------
Surrogate Coordinator: [Name, phone, email]
Communications Director: [Name, phone, email]
Emergency/Crisis Contact: [Name, phone — 24/7]

Reporting: After each event or interaction, text the surrogate
coordinator with: event name, approximate attendance, any
notable conversations, any media interactions, any questions
you couldn't answer.
============================================================
```

## Surrogate-Powered Tactics

### Proxy Canvassing
Surrogates knock doors on behalf of the candidate, opening with their personal connection: "Hi, I'm [name], your neighbor. I'm here because I support [candidate] and wanted to share why." This is especially effective with Tier 2 surrogates who are known in the community.

**Best practices:**
- Pair surrogates with experienced canvassers for their first shift
- Give surrogates a simplified script that lets their personality come through
- Target turf near the surrogate's home or workplace
- Debrief after each shift to capture voter feedback

### Surrogate Town Halls
The surrogate hosts a community conversation on a topic aligned with the campaign's platform. The candidate does not attend — the surrogate is the star.

**Format:**
- 45-60 minutes: 15 min surrogate presentation, 30 min Q&A, 15 min sign-up/mingle
- Ideal venue: community center, library meeting room, church fellowship hall, living room
- Surrogate presents on a topic they are an authority on (e.g., a teacher on education policy)
- Campaign provides talking points connecting the topic to the candidate's platform
- Staff or volunteer captures attendee contact information and notable questions

### Social Media Takeovers
A surrogate takes over the campaign's social media account for a day or specific event, sharing their perspective on why they support the candidate.

**Best practices:**
- Pre-plan content calendar with the surrogate
- Provide brand guidelines and hashtags
- Review posts before they go live (for Tier 2 and Tier 3; Tier 1 may require lighter touch)
- Film short video testimonials for ongoing use

### Letter Writing
Surrogates write letters to the editor, postcards to voters, or personal letters to their networks.

**Templates to provide:**
- Letter to the editor (250 words, personal story + endorsement + call to action)
- Postcard to voters (3-4 sentences, personal and specific)
- Personal email to friends (informal, includes link to donate or volunteer)

### Introduction Calls
Surrogates make personal phone calls to key contacts introducing them to the candidate or campaign.

**Script framework:**
1. Personal greeting and reason for calling
2. "I'm supporting [candidate] because..."
3. One specific thing the candidate will do that matters to this person
4. The ask: "Would you be willing to [attend an event / meet the candidate / learn more]?"
5. Follow-up commitment

## Tracking and Management

### Surrogate Database
Maintain a centralized tracker (spreadsheet or CRM) with:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Name | Full name |
| Tier | 1, 2, or 3 |
| Contact info | Phone, email, preferred contact method |
| Location | Where they are based / communities they reach |
| Expertise/Profile | What makes them credible (title, background) |
| Audiences | Which voter segments they connect with |
| Availability | Days/times available, blackout dates |
| Deployments | Log of every event/action they have completed |
| Performance notes | How they performed, audience feedback |
| Briefing status | Date of last briefing, version of materials received |
| Vetting status | Vetted / pending / flagged |

### Deployment Scheduling Matrix

Match surrogates to events based on:

| Factor | Consideration |
|---|---|
| Audience demographics | Does the surrogate reflect or resonate with this audience? |
| Issue alignment | Is the surrogate credible on the topic being discussed? |
| Geography | Is the surrogate local to the event area? |
| Event scale | Tier 1 for large/media events, Tier 2-3 for community events |
| Availability | Respect surrogate time; avoid burnout |
| Strategic timing | Save high-profile surrogates for high-impact moments |

### Monthly Surrogate Management Cadence

- **Week 1:** Update briefing documents with latest messaging and Q&A
- **Week 2:** Outreach to recruit new surrogates (target 2-3 new Tier 2, 5-10 new Tier 3 per month)
- **Week 3:** Surrogate appreciation (personal thank-you calls, recognition on social media)
- **Week 4:** Review deployment data, assess performance, plan next month's schedule

### Surrogate Communication

- **Group text or messaging channel:** Quick updates, event opportunities, breaking news responses
- **Monthly email update:** Campaign progress, updated talking points, upcoming opportunities
- **Quarterly call or meeting:** Deeper briefing, Q&A with candidate or campaign manager, community of practice

---

## Measuring Surrogate Program Success

- Number of surrogate events completed per month
- Voter contacts generated through surrogate activities
- Media hits attributed to surrogate appearances
- Volunteer sign-ups and donations from surrogate-hosted events
- Social media reach from surrogate content
- Surrogate retention rate (are they staying engaged?)
- Net new surrogates recruited per month

---

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Over-relying on Tier 1 surrogates.** They generate buzz but Tier 2 and Tier 3 move votes through trusted relationships.
2. **Failing to brief surrogates.** An unbriefed surrogate is a liability. Never deploy anyone who hasn't received current materials.
3. **Not tracking deployments.** If you don't know who went where, you can't optimize or follow up.
4. **Ignoring surrogate wellness.** Volunteers burn out. Check in personally, say thank you, and don't overextend any single person.
5. **Treating surrogates as interchangeable.** Match the right person to the right audience. A CEO at a union hall is a mismatch.
