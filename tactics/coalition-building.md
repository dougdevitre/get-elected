# Coalition Building: Engaging Voter Groups to Win

Coalitions win elections. No candidate wins with a single demographic — you win by assembling a coalition of groups who each have their own reasons to support you. This module covers how to reach, message to, and organize twelve key voter groups.

---

## Core Principles

1. **Listen before you message.** Spend twice as long listening to a community as you do talking to them.
2. **Go through trusted messengers.** A candidate showing up cold at a community meeting is tourism. A candidate introduced by a respected leader is a guest.
3. **Deliver something before you ask for something.** Attend their events, amplify their causes, write a letter supporting their issue — then ask for support.
4. **Tailor the message, not the values.** Your core platform stays the same. The emphasis, language, and examples shift for each audience.
5. **Follow through.** Broken promises to coalition partners travel faster than kept ones.

## Parents and Families

**How to reach:** PTA/PTO meetings, school board meetings, youth sports leagues, daycare and preschool networks, parenting Facebook groups, pediatrician waiting rooms (with permission), back-to-school events, playground conversations.

**Key issues:** School quality and funding, childcare costs and availability, school safety, homework and testing policies, after-school programs, property taxes (which fund schools), parks and recreation, traffic safety near schools.

**Trusted messengers:** Popular teachers, PTA presidents, school counselors, pediatricians, youth coaches, parent bloggers/influencers.

**Events:** Education town halls, candidate read-aloud at libraries, "Parents for [Candidate]" coffee mornings, school supply drive partnerships.

**Messaging do's:**
- Lead with your own experience as a parent, or your connection to children in the community
- Be specific about education policy — parents know when you are faking it
- Acknowledge the real pressures: cost, time, anxiety about their kids' futures

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not reduce parents to a single issue — they care about more than schools
- Do not assume all parents agree on education policy (school choice is divisive)
- Do not lecture parents about what is best for their children

**Organizations:** Local PTA councils, parent advocacy groups, childcare provider associations, youth sports leagues.

## Seniors (65+)

**How to reach:** Senior centers, churches, VFW and American Legion posts, Rotary and Kiwanis clubs, community dances and social events, AARP chapter meetings, hospital and pharmacy bulletin boards, local newspaper ads and letters, AM radio.

**Key issues:** Social Security and Medicare protection, prescription drug costs, property tax relief, public safety, accessible transportation, healthcare access, isolation and community connection, legacy and community preservation.

**Trusted messengers:** Their peers (other seniors), faith leaders, longtime community residents, local doctors, adult children who are campaign supporters.

**Events:** Candidate coffee at the senior center, intergenerational town halls, health care listening sessions, "meet the candidate" luncheons.

**Messaging do's:**
- Speak slowly, clearly, and with respect — never condescension
- Emphasize experience, stability, and fiscal responsibility
- Connect your platform to protecting what they have built
- Use physical mail — seniors read their mail

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not assume seniors are technologically illiterate — many are on Facebook
- Do not patronize or use "silver tsunami" type language
- Do not ignore their concerns about change — acknowledge what they value about how things are

**Organizations:** AARP chapters, senior centers, Area Agency on Aging, retired teachers associations, Gray Panthers.

## Veterans and Military Families

**How to reach:** VFW and American Legion posts, Veterans of Foreign Wars events, Memorial Day and Veterans Day ceremonies, military base community events (where applicable), veteran service organizations, veteran-owned businesses.

**Key issues:** VA healthcare access and quality, veteran homelessness, mental health and suicide prevention, employment and job training, education benefits, military family support, national security, honoring service.

**Trusted messengers:** Fellow veterans (especially from the same branch or era), Gold Star families, veteran service organization leaders, retired military officers.

**Events:** Veterans roundtable, visit to VA facility, veteran appreciation event, partnership with veteran service organizations on a community project.

**Messaging do's:**
- If you are a veteran, lead with your service — it is your strongest credential with this group
- If you are not a veteran, be honest about it and show you have done the work to understand their issues
- Be specific about policy, not just "I support our troops"
- Acknowledge the full spectrum of military experience, including the difficult parts

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not use veterans as props — they will see through it immediately
- Do not assume all veterans are politically conservative
- Do not conflate patriotism with a specific policy position
- Do not claim military experience you do not have

**Organizations:** VFW, American Legion, DAV, Team Rubicon, Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America, Student Veterans of America (on campuses).

## Faith Communities

**How to reach:** Worship services (as a visitor, not a campaigner), faith leader one-on-one meetings, interfaith councils, church suppers and community meals, faith-based community service events, denominational gatherings.

**Key issues:** Varies widely by tradition — religious liberty, social justice, poverty, education, family values, immigration, criminal justice reform, environmental stewardship. Ask, do not assume.

**Trusted messengers:** Pastors, rabbis, imams, priests, deacons, church elders, faith-based nonprofit directors.

**Events:** Candidate prayer breakfast, interfaith forum, faith and community town hall, joint community service project.

**Messaging do's:**
- Be authentic about your own faith journey, whatever it is
- Connect your platform to shared moral values (justice, compassion, stewardship, community)
- Respect the sacred nature of worship spaces — never campaign inside a sanctuary uninvited

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not fake religiosity
- Do not assume a congregation is monolithic — every church has political diversity
- Do not ask clergy to endorse from the pulpit (it risks their tax-exempt status)
- Do not quote scripture you do not understand

**Organizations:** Local ministerial alliances, interfaith councils, faith-based community organizing networks (PICO, IAF, Gamaliel), denominational social justice offices.

## Small Business Owners

**How to reach:** Chamber of Commerce meetings, Main Street associations, small business development centers, networking events, restaurant and retail visits (walking the commercial district), local business expos.

**Key issues:** Tax burden, regulatory burden, healthcare costs for employees, commercial rent and property taxes, workforce availability, infrastructure and transportation, parking, local economic development, government contracting access.

**Trusted messengers:** Chamber of Commerce president, successful local entrepreneurs, SCORE mentors, small business development center directors.

**Events:** Small business roundtable, "Shop Local" campaign partnership, candidate walkthrough of business district, economic development town hall.

**Messaging do's:**
- Demonstrate understanding of what it takes to make payroll
- Be specific about what you will do to help small businesses (tax policy, regulatory reform, procurement)
- Visit their businesses — shake hands, buy something, listen to their concerns

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not lump small business with big business — they have different interests
- Do not promise things you cannot deliver (tax cuts that require state or federal action)
- Do not show up only during the campaign — build ongoing relationships

**Organizations:** Local Chamber of Commerce, NFIB chapters, SBA resource partners, minority business associations, industry-specific associations.

## Young Voters (18-29)

**How to reach:** College campuses, apartment complexes near campuses, social media (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube), music venues, coffee shops, young professional networking events, volunteer organizations popular with young people.

**Key issues:** Student debt, housing affordability, climate change, job market and wages, social justice, healthcare access, gun violence, reproductive rights, civic engagement, feeling heard by government.

**Trusted messengers:** Peers (other young people), professors, campus organization leaders, young social media influencers, young elected officials, popular local musicians or artists.

**Events:** Campus town hall, voter registration drive, issues-focused happy hour, "Pizza and Politics" night, volunteer day tied to a cause they care about.

**Messaging do's:**
- Be authentic and direct — young voters have excellent BS detectors
- Meet them on their platforms (short-form video, Instagram stories)
- Focus on how your platform affects their daily life (rent, debt, wages, climate)
- Empower them — "your generation will decide this" is motivating when sincere

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not talk down to them or assume they are naive
- Do not assume they are all liberal — young voters are more ideologically diverse than stereotypes suggest
- Do not ignore their skepticism about politics — acknowledge it and show why this race is different
- Do not use slang you do not naturally use

**Organizations:** Campus Democrats/Republicans, student government, Young Democrats/Republicans, civic engagement nonprofits (CIRCLE, Rock the Vote, NextGen), college media.

## Labor and Union Members

**How to reach:** Union hall meetings, job site visits (with union leader invitation), labor council meetings, labor-endorsed events, picket lines (show solidarity), Labor Day parades.

**Key issues:** Wages and benefits, workplace safety, right to organize, pension protection, trade policy, prevailing wage laws, healthcare costs, job training and apprenticeships, government contracting and project labor agreements.

**Trusted messengers:** Union local presidents, shop stewards, business agents, labor council leaders, rank-and-file members who are respected on the job.

**Events:** Labor endorsement interview, job site tour, workers' town hall, Labor Day event appearance.

**Messaging do's:**
- Understand the difference between public and private sector unions
- Be specific about labor policy — they will ask detailed questions
- Show up at picket lines and solidarity events (actions speak louder than platforms)
- Respect the endorsement process — earn it through the formal channels

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not take labor support for granted
- Do not cross a picket line, ever, under any circumstances during a campaign
- Do not make promises you cannot keep on specific contract negotiations
- Do not assume all union members vote the same way

**Organizations:** AFL-CIO central labor councils, individual union locals (SEIU, AFSCME, IBEW, UA, teachers unions), Building Trades councils, Jobs with Justice.

## Environmentalists

**How to reach:** Environmental organization meetings, outdoor recreation groups, farmers markets, sustainability fairs, clean energy events, local conservation projects, Sierra Club outings.

**Key issues:** Climate change, clean air and water, renewable energy, conservation and public lands, environmental justice, sustainable development, pesticide and chemical regulation, wildlife protection.

**Trusted messengers:** Local environmental leaders, conservation biologists, outdoor recreation business owners, clean energy entrepreneurs, environmental justice activists, respected farmers.

**Events:** Trail cleanup or park restoration, environmental policy forum, clean energy business tour, water quality town hall.

**Messaging do's:**
- Connect environmental policy to economic opportunity (clean energy jobs, tourism, property values)
- Be specific about local environmental issues (this watershed, this air quality concern)
- Acknowledge environmental justice — pollution disproportionately affects low-income communities

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not dismiss climate science even if some voters are skeptical — find common ground on clean air and water
- Do not make the environment an afterthought — for this community, it is the issue
- Do not greenwash — environmentalists will fact-check your record

**Organizations:** Sierra Club chapters, local land trusts, Audubon Society, League of Conservation Voters, environmental justice organizations, watershed associations.

## Immigrant Communities

**How to reach:** Cultural community organizations, houses of worship serving immigrant populations, ethnic grocery stores and restaurants, ESL classes, citizenship preparation programs, community cultural festivals, ethnic media (newspapers, radio, TV in relevant languages).

**Key issues:** Immigration policy (varies by status and community), language access in government services, education, healthcare access, workplace protections, anti-discrimination, community safety (without fear of deportation), small business support, cultural preservation.

**Trusted messengers:** Established community leaders, bilingual professionals, immigrant business owners, faith leaders serving immigrant congregations, longtime residents who are respected bridges between communities.

**Events:** Multilingual town hall, cultural festival participation (as a guest, not a star), citizenship ceremony attendance, partnership with community organizations on service projects.

**Messaging do's:**
- Provide materials in relevant languages — this is respect, not a luxury
- Acknowledge the contributions immigrants make to the community
- Be specific about policies that affect their daily lives (not just federal immigration policy)
- Listen to concerns about local government accessibility

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not treat immigrant communities as monolithic — Cuban Americans, Somali Americans, and Vietnamese Americans have very different concerns
- Do not assume everyone in the community is an immigrant or wants to talk about immigration
- Do not show up only during campaigns — build relationships year-round
- Do not make promises about federal immigration policy you have no power to deliver at the local level

**Organizations:** Immigrant mutual aid societies, cultural associations, legal aid organizations, refugee resettlement agencies, ethnic chambers of commerce.

## Rural Communities

**How to reach:** Diner and coffee shop conversations, feed stores and farm supply businesses, county fairs, livestock auctions, church suppers, town board meetings, volunteer fire department events, high school sports events.

**Key issues:** Agricultural policy, broadband access, healthcare (hospital closures), school consolidation, infrastructure (roads, bridges, water systems), property taxes, land use and zoning, opioid crisis, economic development, maintaining community identity.

**Trusted messengers:** Longtime farmers, rural business owners, volunteer fire chiefs, school principals, county extension agents, church pastors.

**Messaging do's:**
- Show up in person — repeatedly — rural voters judge you by your presence
- Be honest about what government can and cannot do
- Respect the self-reliance ethos while explaining how policy can remove barriers
- Know the local economy — what do people here actually do for work?

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not be a stranger who shows up once — that confirms the "only comes when they need votes" stereotype
- Do not talk about rural communities like they are problems to be solved
- Do not ignore rural in favor of higher-density areas just because the math is harder

## Suburban Voters

**How to reach:** HOA meetings, school events, youth sports, commuter transit stations, community Facebook groups, farmers markets, shopping centers, neighborhood parks.

**Key issues:** Property taxes, school quality, traffic and commute times, public safety, property values, local development and density, healthcare costs, quality of life.

**Trusted messengers:** Neighborhood association leaders, school parents, local business owners, community volunteers, HOA board members.

**Messaging do's:**
- Focus on quality-of-life issues — that is what drew them to the suburbs
- Be specific about local concerns (the intersection that needs a light, the school that needs renovation)
- Acknowledge that suburban voters are diverse in every way

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not treat suburban voters as boring or apolitical — they are the swing voters who decide most elections
- Do not ignore the growing diversity of suburbs

---

## Urban Voters

**How to reach:** Neighborhood association meetings, community festivals, transit stops, barbershops and beauty salons, community centers, block parties, tenant association meetings, union halls, social media, local arts events.

**Key issues:** Affordable housing, public safety, transit, education funding, gentrification, environmental justice, jobs and wages, healthcare access, police accountability, infrastructure.

**Trusted messengers:** Block captains, tenant organizers, neighborhood association presidents, barbers and salon owners, community organization leaders, faith leaders.

**Messaging do's:**
- Be present and consistent — show up at community meetings, not just rallies
- Acknowledge systemic challenges honestly
- Connect policy to lived experience in specific neighborhoods

**Messaging don'ts:**
- Do not conflate urban with a single race or ethnicity
- Do not promise transformation without explaining how
- Do not ignore longtime residents in favor of newer, louder constituencies

---

## Coalition Assembly Checklist

1. Map every voter group in your district by size and alignment
2. Identify 2-3 trusted messengers per group
3. Build relationships with community leaders before making asks
4. Develop group-specific messaging within your overall platform
5. Assign a campaign staffer or volunteer captain to each coalition group
6. Hold at least one event specifically for each target group
7. Include coalition group members in visible campaign roles (advisory committees, endorsement announcements, event speakers)
8. Track engagement and voter contact by group
9. Follow up after the election regardless of outcome — coalitions outlast campaigns
