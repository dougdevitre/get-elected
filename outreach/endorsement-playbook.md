# Endorsement Playbook: Earning, Announcing, and Leveraging Endorsements

Endorsements are social proof at scale. A single endorsement from the right person can move an entire voter segment. A well-timed endorsement announcement can dominate a news cycle. A poorly managed endorsement program wastes political capital and creates resentment.

---

## Why Endorsements Matter

1. **Credibility transfer.** Voters who trust the endorser transfer that trust to the candidate.
2. **Media hooks.** Endorsements give reporters a reason to cover the campaign.
3. **Voter cues.** In low-information races, endorsements are the primary decision shortcut for many voters.
4. **Volunteer recruitment.** People follow leaders they admire into action.
5. **Fundraising leverage.** An endorser's network becomes an accessible donor pool.
6. **Opponent demoralization.** Stacking endorsements signals inevitability.

Not all endorsements are equal. A newspaper endorsement in a local race can swing 2-5% of undecided voters. An endorsement from a popular local figure can move a specific community. A national figure's endorsement generates attention but may not move local votes.

---

## Tiered Targeting

### Tier 1: Elected Officials and Major Political Figures

**Who:** Current and former officeholders, party chairs, prominent political figures.
**Value:** Highest credibility, media coverage, fundraising access, organizational support.
**Difficulty:** High — they consider political risk, competing loyalties, and future implications.
**Approach:** Candidate-to-candidate, personal relationship, clear mutual benefit.

### Tier 2: Organizations and Institutions

**Who:** Labor unions, advocacy organizations, professional associations, newspapers, civic groups.
**Value:** Institutional credibility, voter guides, organizational infrastructure, earned media.
**Difficulty:** Medium to high — formal processes, questionnaires, interviews, board votes.
**Approach:** Research their process early, complete questionnaires thoroughly, prepare for interviews.

### Tier 3: Community Leaders

**Who:** Business owners, faith leaders, educators, nonprofit directors, coaches, neighborhood leaders.
**Value:** Grassroots credibility, community-specific trust, peer influence.
**Difficulty:** Low to medium — personal relationship and issue alignment usually sufficient.
**Approach:** Campaign manager or candidate direct outreach, relationship-first.

### Tier 4: Prominent Individuals and Influencers

**Who:** Local celebrities, social media influencers, respected professionals, cultural figures.
**Value:** Audience reach, cultural credibility, content generation.
**Difficulty:** Low to medium — often flattered to be asked, may need education on issues.
**Approach:** Identify through social listening, personal connection, or mutual contacts.

## Prioritization Matrix

Score every prospective endorser on two axes:

**Influence (1-5):**
- 5: Moves thousands of voters or unlocks major resources
- 4: Moves hundreds of voters or a key demographic
- 3: Respected voice in a specific community
- 2: Known locally, modest following
- 1: Supportive individual with limited reach

**Likelihood (1-5):**
- 5: Already supportive, just needs to be asked
- 4: Favorable, needs a conversation
- 3: Open-minded, needs persuasion
- 2: Leaning elsewhere, possible with effort
- 1: Unlikely or committed to opponent

**Priority tiers:**
- **Immediate (Score 8-10):** High influence + high likelihood. Pursue within the first two weeks.
- **Strategic (Score 6-7):** Worth investing time. Schedule a meeting within 30 days.
- **Opportunistic (Score 4-5):** Pursue if circumstances align. Do not over-invest.
- **Monitor (Score 2-3):** Keep the door open. Revisit if dynamics shift.

## The Ask

### Principles

1. **Ask in person or by phone.** Never make the initial endorsement ask by email or text.
2. **The candidate makes the ask for Tier 1.** Campaign manager or political director for Tier 2-4.
3. **Be direct.** "I am asking for your endorsement" is clearer than "I'd love your support."
4. **Explain why their endorsement specifically matters.** Flattery that is specific is persuasion. Flattery that is generic is noise.
5. **Give them an out.** "I understand if the timing isn't right" preserves the relationship for future asks.
6. **Confirm what "endorsement" means.** Public statement? Name on materials? Active campaigning? Set expectations.

### Phone Script (Candidate to Prospective Endorser)

"[Name], thank you for taking my call. I've always respected your [specific thing — leadership on X issue, your work at Y organization, your voice in this community]. I'm running for [office] because [one sentence motivation], and your endorsement would mean a great deal to me.

Here's why I'm asking you specifically: [reason their endorsement matters — audience they reach, credibility they carry, issue alignment]. I believe we share a commitment to [shared value].

I want to be upfront about what I'm asking. I'd love to be able to announce your endorsement publicly, include your name on our campaign materials, and if you're willing, have you say a few words at [specific event or medium]. But I'm also happy to discuss what you're comfortable with.

What questions do you have for me?"

### In-Person Meeting Framework

1. **Personal connection** (5 min): Relationship building, show you did your homework
2. **Campaign overview** (5 min): Why you are running, path to victory, current momentum
3. **The ask** (2 min): Direct, specific, explain why they matter
4. **Their concerns** (10 min): Listen more than you talk. Address honestly.
5. **Terms and timeline** (5 min): What endorsement looks like, when you need an answer
6. **Next steps** (3 min): Confirm follow-up, thank them regardless of answer

### Written Request (for Organizational Endorsements)

Include:
- Cover letter from candidate with personal connection to organization's mission
- Completed questionnaire (fill every question, do not skip or hedge)
- Campaign bio and fact sheet
- List of other endorsements received (social proof)
- Specific policy commitments relevant to the organization
- Availability for interview or forum appearance

## Activation: Announcing and Leveraging Endorsements

### Announcement Sequencing

Do not announce all endorsements at once. Space them strategically:

1. **Launch phase:** 3-5 anchor endorsements announced at campaign launch or within the first week. These set the tone and signal viability.
2. **Momentum phase:** Release endorsements in clusters of 2-3 every 1-2 weeks to sustain news cycles.
3. **Thematic drops:** Group endorsements by theme: "20 local business owners endorse [Candidate]" or "Law enforcement leaders back [Candidate]."
4. **Counter-programming:** Hold a major endorsement in reserve to deploy when the opponent gets a positive news cycle.
5. **Final push:** Save 2-3 high-profile endorsements for the last two weeks when voter attention peaks.

### Press Release Template

**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**
[Date]

**[Endorser Title and Name] Endorses [Candidate] for [Office]**

[CITY] — [Endorser full name and title] today announced [his/her/their] endorsement of [Candidate] for [Office].

"[Quote from endorser — 2-3 sentences, personal and specific about why they support the candidate]," said [Endorser last name].

[One paragraph of context: endorser's background, relevance to the race, significance of the endorsement.]

"[Quote from candidate — 1-2 sentences expressing gratitude and shared commitment]," said [Candidate last name].

[Candidate] has also been endorsed by [list of other notable endorsements for social proof].

[Boilerplate: candidate bio, election date, website.]

**Media contact:** [Name, phone, email]

### Social Media Activation

- **Graphic:** Quote card with endorser's photo, their quote, and campaign branding
- **Video:** 30-60 second endorser testimonial (selfie-style is fine for authenticity)
- **Post copy:** "[Endorser] is backing [Candidate] because [reason in endorser's words]. We're honored. [Link to full statement]"
- **Endorser's channels:** Ask the endorser to share from their own accounts. Provide them with copy and graphics they can use as-is.
- **Tag and thank:** Tag the endorser, their organization, and any relevant community pages

### Voter Guide Integration

List endorsements on:
- Campaign website endorsements page (organized by category)
- Palm cards and walk literature
- Direct mail pieces targeting relevant voter segments
- Digital ads targeting the endorser's community or issue area

## Maintaining Endorser Engagement

Endorsers who feel forgotten after the announcement become resentful. Keep them engaged:

1. **Monthly update email** to all endorsers: campaign progress, how their endorsement is being used, upcoming opportunities
2. **Invite to key events:** Give endorsers VIP treatment at rallies, fundraisers, and election night
3. **Activate for specific asks:** "Would you be willing to call [person] / write a letter to the editor / attend [event]?"
4. **Thank publicly and repeatedly:** Social media shoutouts, acknowledgment at events, handwritten notes
5. **Report back on impact:** "Your endorsement was featured in our mailer to 15,000 households in your community"

## Tracking Spreadsheet Schema

Maintain a master endorsement tracker with the following fields:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Name | Full name of endorser |
| Title/Role | Current position or description |
| Tier | 1, 2, 3, or 4 |
| Influence Score | 1-5 |
| Likelihood Score | 1-5 |
| Priority | Immediate / Strategic / Opportunistic / Monitor |
| Status | Prospect / Asked / Confirmed / Declined / Public |
| Ask Date | Date endorsement was requested |
| Ask Method | Phone / In-person / Written |
| Asked By | Candidate / Campaign Manager / Other |
| Confirmation Date | Date endorser confirmed |
| Announcement Date | Date endorsement was made public |
| Announcement Channel | Press release / Social / Event / Mail |
| Terms | What they agreed to (name only, active, surrogate, etc.) |
| Follow-up Owner | Staff member responsible for relationship |
| Last Contact | Date of most recent communication |
| Notes | Context, concerns raised, conditions, connections |

### Workflow

1. Build prospect list using community mapping, political network analysis, and campaign research
2. Score and prioritize every prospect
3. Assign outreach responsibility and deadline
4. Track every interaction in the spreadsheet
5. Move confirmed endorsers into the announcement pipeline
6. Schedule announcements using the sequencing strategy above
7. Transition announced endorsers into the surrogate program for ongoing activation

## Common Mistakes

1. **Asking too late.** Start endorsement outreach the day you decide to run. Early endorsements freeze out opponents.
2. **Not following up.** Many endorsers say "let me think about it" and genuinely mean it. Follow up within a week.
3. **Announcing without permission.** Always confirm the exact language and timing with the endorser before going public.
4. **Treating endorsements as trophies.** An endorsement sitting on a website does nothing. Activate it through voter contact, media, and digital.
5. **Ignoring declined asks.** A "no" today may become a "yes" later. Thank them, keep the door open, and revisit after a momentum shift.
6. **Failing to prepare endorsers.** Once someone endorses, they will be asked about it. Brief them with talking points.
7. **Over-indexing on quantity.** Fifty unknown names on a list impress no one. Five respected voices in the right communities move votes.
