# Debate Prep

A comprehensive guide for preparing for, executing, and capitalizing on candidate debates. Covers opponent research, message pillar development, pivot techniques, hostile question preparation, logistics, and post-debate strategy.

---

## Pre-Debate Preparation Timeline

| Timeframe | Action |
|---|---|
| 3-4 weeks before | Complete opponent research dossier |
| 2-3 weeks before | Identify 3 message pillars and prepare pivots |
| 2 weeks before | Begin mock debate sessions (at least 3 sessions) |
| 1 week before | Prepare answers to 20 common questions (see below) |
| 3 days before | Final mock debate; refine opening/closing statements |
| Day before | Light review only; rest; confirm logistics |
| Day of | Arrive early; walk the stage; final briefing |

---

## Opponent Research for Debates

Before you can debate your opponent, you must know their record, positions, weaknesses, and likely attacks better than they do.

### Research Checklist

```
VOTING RECORD (if incumbent or former officeholder):
[ ] Every vote on your three core issues
[ ] Missed votes (attendance record)
[ ] Votes that contradict their current positions
[ ] Votes against popular measures

PUBLIC STATEMENTS:
[ ] Past debate performances (watch video; note patterns)
[ ] Newspaper quotes and op-eds
[ ] Social media posts (especially old or deleted ones)
[ ] Campaign website positions
[ ] Endorsement questionnaire responses

FINANCIAL:
[ ] Top donors and industries (what interests fund them?)
[ ] Personal financial disclosures
[ ] Business dealings that create conflicts of interest

PERSONAL/PROFESSIONAL:
[ ] Resume claims that can be verified or challenged
[ ] Lawsuits, legal issues, ethics complaints
[ ] Organizational memberships and affiliations

DEBATE TENDENCIES (from prior debate footage):
[ ] Do they get flustered when challenged?
[ ] Do they pivot well or ramble?
[ ] Do they attack first or wait?
[ ] What is their body language when uncomfortable?
[ ] What is their go-to deflection?
```

## Three Message Pillars

Your entire debate performance should revolve around three pillars — the same three issues from your positioning framework. Every answer should connect back to one of these pillars.

### Pillar Template

```
PILLAR 1: [Issue Name]
  Key message: [One sentence]
  Supporting fact: [Statistic or specific example]
  Story: [15-second anecdote about a real person]
  Contrast: [Opponent's record on this issue]
  Pivot phrase: "And that's exactly why [Issue] is so important..."

PILLAR 2: [Issue Name]
  Key message: [One sentence]
  Supporting fact: [Statistic or specific example]
  Story: [15-second anecdote]
  Contrast: [Opponent's record]
  Pivot phrase: "This connects directly to [Issue]..."

PILLAR 3: [Issue Name]
  Key message: [One sentence]
  Supporting fact: [Statistic or specific example]
  Story: [15-second anecdote]
  Contrast: [Opponent's record]
  Pivot phrase: "The families I talk to care about [Issue]..."
```

## Pivot Techniques

A pivot is how you move from the question asked to the answer you want to give. Every politician does this; great debaters do it seamlessly.

### The Three-Step Pivot

```
1. ACKNOWLEDGE the question (2-5 seconds)
   "That's a fair question" / "I'm glad you raised that" / 
   "Voters deserve an answer on that"

2. BRIDGE to your message (5-10 seconds)
   "And it connects to something I hear every day..."
   "What's really at the heart of this is..."
   "Here's what matters most about that..."

3. DELIVER your message pillar (20-30 seconds)
   [Your prepared talking point on your chosen issue]
```

### Bridge Phrases

```
"The real question is..."
"What voters are really asking is..."
"Let me put this in perspective..."
"This is exactly why I'm running..."
"Here's what that means for families in this district..."
"My opponent wants to talk about [X], but voters care about [Y]..."
"That's an important issue, and it's connected to [your pillar]..."
```

### When NOT to Pivot

```
- When the question is directly about your biggest vulnerability 
  (pivoting looks evasive — address it head-on, then pivot)
- When the moderator explicitly says "please answer the question"
- When the audience clearly cares about this topic and expects a 
  direct answer
- On yes/no questions (answer yes or no FIRST, then expand)
```

## Hostile Question Preparation

Prepare for the worst questions. If you have rehearsed the hardest possible question, nothing in the actual debate will surprise you.

### Hostile Question Response Framework

```
1. DO NOT SHOW SURPRISE OR ANGER. Breathe. Pause one beat.
2. REFRAME the question if it contains a false premise.
   "I'd push back on the premise of that question. The reality is..."
3. GIVE A DIRECT ANSWER if possible — then explain.
4. PIVOT to your message pillar.
5. NEVER REPEAT a negative frame. If asked "Why are you soft on crime?"
   do NOT say "I'm not soft on crime." Say "I'm committed to keeping 
   our community safe. Here's my plan..."
```

## 20 Common Debate Questions

Prepare a 60-90 second answer for each of these. Practice until you can deliver them smoothly.

**On Your Candidacy:**
1. Why are you running for this office?
2. What qualifies you for this position?
3. What is the single most important issue in this race?
4. What would you do in your first 30 days in office?
5. Why should voters choose you over your opponent?

**On Issues:**
6. What is your plan to address [top local issue — e.g., property taxes, housing, crime]?
7. How would you balance the budget without cutting essential services?
8. What is your position on [hot-button national issue — e.g., immigration, guns, abortion]?
9. How will you address the rising cost of living for families in this district?
10. What would you do about [recent local crisis or controversy]?

**On Your Record/Background:**
11. You have no political experience. Why should voters trust you?
12. You have been in office for [X] years. Why haven't you fixed this already?
13. Your opponent says [specific attack]. How do you respond?
14. You accepted donations from [controversial donor/industry]. Why?
15. You changed your position on [issue]. Why?

**Traps and Curveballs:**
16. Name one thing your opponent is right about.
17. What is your biggest weakness as a candidate?
18. Will you commit to [specific pledge the moderator names]?
19. Do you support your party's national leadership? (Designed to make you choose between base and swing voters)
20. What would you say to voters in the other party who are considering voting for you?

### How to Handle "Name One Nice Thing About Your Opponent"

```
This is a common debate question designed to test grace. Prepare 
a genuine but strategically neutral answer:

"I respect that [opponent] has [neutral positive — e.g., 'dedicated 
years to public service' or 'cares about this community']. Where we 
disagree is on [pivot to your contrast]. And that disagreement is 
what this election is about."

DO NOT: Refuse to answer (looks petty)
DO NOT: Be too generous (undermines your contrast case)
DO: Give a brief, genuine compliment and pivot immediately
```

## Opening and Closing Statements

### Opening Statement (60-90 seconds typical)

```
Structure:
- Greeting (5 seconds)
- Hook — who you are and why you're here (15 seconds)
- Your three issues in one sentence each (30 seconds)
- Contrast frame (15 seconds)
- Forward-looking statement (10 seconds)

Template:
"Good evening. I'm [Name], and I'm running for [Office] because 
[one-sentence motivation].

The families I talk to every day are worried about three things: 
[Issue 1], [Issue 2], and [Issue 3].

On each of these issues, there's a clear choice in this election. 
[One sentence contrast].

Over the next [time], I look forward to sharing my vision for 
[community] — and I hope to earn your vote."
```

### Closing Statement (60-90 seconds typical)

The close is the last thing voters hear. It must be memorable, emotional, and end with a clear call to action.

```
Structure:
- Reference a story or moment from the debate (10 seconds)
- Restate your vision (20 seconds)
- Emotional appeal (20 seconds)
- Call to action with specific date and website (20 seconds)

Template:
"Tonight you've heard two very different visions for [community]. 

I believe [vision statement — what the community can be].

I've spent this campaign listening — at doors, at diners, at 
community meetings. What I've heard is that people are ready 
for [your core theme: change/leadership/accountability].

On [Election Day date], you have the power to make that happen. 
I'm asking for your vote. Visit [website] to learn more. 
Together, we will [vision]. Thank you."
```

## Night-of Logistics

### Pre-Debate Checklist

```
ARRIVAL (1-2 hours before):
[ ] Arrive early to walk the stage/room
[ ] Check podium height, microphone, lighting
[ ] Identify where the cameras will be
[ ] Identify friendly faces in the audience for eye contact
[ ] Confirm format: time limits, topics, rebuttal rules

WHAT TO BRING:
[ ] One notepad and pen (for jotting notes during opponent's answers)
[ ] Water bottle (stay hydrated; dry mouth kills delivery)
[ ] One-page cheat sheet of key facts and statistics (if allowed)
[ ] Phone on silent (give to staff before going on stage)

WHAT TO WEAR:
[ ] Solid colors (no busy patterns — they strobe on camera)
[ ] Dark suit/blazer for TV; business casual for community forum
[ ] No jingling jewelry or noisy accessories
[ ] Comfortable shoes (you may be standing for 60-90 minutes)

FINAL MENTAL PREP:
[ ] Review your three pillars and pivot phrases
[ ] Review your opening and closing statements
[ ] Take 5 minutes alone to breathe and center yourself
[ ] Remember: you are having a conversation, not performing
```

## Post-Debate Spin

The narrative of "who won the debate" is often set in the 30 minutes after it ends, not during the debate itself.

### Post-Debate Action Plan

```
IMMEDIATELY AFTER (0-30 minutes):
[ ] Candidate goes to the spin room / media area
[ ] Prepared surrogates engage reporters with talking points
[ ] Campaign sends rapid-response email to press list: 
    "[Candidate] Wins Debate on [Issue]"
[ ] Social media team posts highlight clips within 15 minutes

WITHIN 2 HOURS:
[ ] Email blast to supporters: "I just finished the debate..."
    (fundraising ask included)
[ ] Full video or key clips posted to YouTube and social media
[ ] Press release with key quotes and fact-checks of opponent

NEXT MORNING:
[ ] Follow-up earned media pitch: "Debate showed clear contrast on [Issue]"
[ ] Social media highlights throughout the day
[ ] Volunteer talking points updated with debate moments
[ ] Canvass script updated if debate generated a strong contrast moment
```

### Spin Talking Points Template

```
Prepare three talking points for surrogates and press:

1. "[Candidate] won on [Issue]: [specific moment or quote]"
2. "[Opponent] couldn't answer on [weakness]: [specific dodge or gaffe]"
3. "The clear choice: [one-sentence contrast that emerged from debate]"
```
