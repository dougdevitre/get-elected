# Positioning Framework

A systematic approach to developing your campaign's core message. This framework uses the Message Box model to define your positioning relative to your opponent, guides issue selection, and enforces message discipline across all communications.

---

## The Message Box

The Message Box is a four-quadrant framework developed by political strategist Tully Plesser. Every campaign operates in all four quadrants simultaneously. Your job is to control as many quadrants as possible.

```
┌─────────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┐
│                             │                             │
│   WHAT WE SAY ABOUT US     │   WHAT WE SAY ABOUT THEM   │
│                             │                             │
│   (Our positive message)    │   (Our contrast/attack)     │
│                             │                             │
│   This is your proactive    │   This is where you draw    │
│   case — why voters should  │   distinctions — why your   │
│   choose YOU.               │   opponent is the wrong     │
│                             │   choice.                   │
│                             │                             │
├─────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────┤
│                             │                             │
│   WHAT THEY SAY ABOUT THEM │   WHAT THEY SAY ABOUT US   │
│                             │                             │
│   (Their positive message)  │   (Their attacks on us)     │
│                             │                             │
│   Understand their case so  │   Anticipate attacks so     │
│   you can neutralize or     │   you can inoculate and     │
│   co-opt it.                │   prepare responses.        │
│                             │                             │
└─────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────┘
```

## Issue Selection

### The Rule of Three

Voters remember at most three things about a candidate. Pick three issues and hammer them relentlessly. Every speech, ad, door knock, and social media post should connect back to your three issues.

### Issue Selection Criteria

Rate each potential issue on these five factors (1-5 scale):

```
Issue: _____________________________

1. SALIENCE: Do voters in this district care deeply about this?     [ /5]
   (Check polling, local news, town hall feedback, canvass data)

2. CREDIBILITY: Is our candidate credible on this issue?            [ /5]
   (Background, experience, track record, personal story)

3. CONTRAST: Does this issue create a clear distinction             [ /5]
   with our opponent?
   (Voting record, public statements, donors, positions)

4. BREADTH: Does this issue appeal across our target coalition?     [ /5]
   (Base + persuadable voters — not just one group)

5. ACTIONABILITY: Can our candidate offer a concrete, believable    [ /5]
   solution at this level of office?
   (A city council candidate cannot promise to fix Social Security)

TOTAL: [ /25]
```

Select the three issues with the highest total scores.

## Message Discipline

Message discipline means staying on message in every interaction — speeches, interviews, debates, social media, canvass scripts, fundraising emails, and casual conversations.

### The Message Discipline Rules

```
1. EVERY public communication must connect to at least one of your 
   three issues. If it does not, cut it or rewrite it.

2. NEVER introduce a new issue unprompted. If a reporter asks about 
   an issue outside your three, answer briefly and PIVOT back.

3. USE THE SAME LANGUAGE every time. Repetition is how messages 
   penetrate. If your issue is "property tax relief," always call 
   it "property tax relief" — not "tax reform" one day and 
   "fiscal responsibility" the next.

4. EVERY STAFF MEMBER AND VOLUNTEER uses the same message. 
   Distribute a one-page message card. Quiz volunteers on it.

5. DO NOT REACT to every opponent attack. Only respond when the 
   attack threatens to redefine you. Otherwise, pivot back to 
   your message.

6. THE CANDIDATE sets the message; the message does not change 
   based on the audience. Tone and emphasis may shift, but the 
   core message stays the same at a union hall and a chamber of 
   commerce meeting.
```

### The Pivot Technique

When asked about an off-message topic, use a pivot to return to your three issues:

```
ACKNOWLEDGE → BRIDGE → MESSAGE

"That's an important question. [ACKNOWLEDGE]
 What I hear from voters across this district is... [BRIDGE]
 ...that they need [YOUR ISSUE]. That's why I'm fighting for 
 [YOUR POSITION]." [MESSAGE]
```

**Pivot phrases (bridges):**
- "What matters most to the families I talk to is..."
- "The real issue here is..."
- "Let me tell you what I'm focused on..."
- "What's really at stake in this election is..."
- "Here's what I think is most important..."

## Contrast Messaging

Contrast is not the same as attack. Contrast draws a factual distinction between candidates. Attack is personal. Effective campaigns use contrast; reckless campaigns use attack.

### The Contrast Formula

```
[PROBLEM that voters feel] + 
[OPPONENT's record/position that made it worse or failed to fix it] + 
[YOUR specific plan/position that addresses it] = 
EFFECTIVE CONTRAST
```

### Contrast Examples

```
WEAK (pure attack, no positive):
"My opponent voted against the school budget."

STRONG (contrast with positive alternative):
"When the school budget was on the line, my opponent voted no — 
putting our kids' education at risk. I'll fight for full funding 
for our schools because every child in this district deserves 
a quality education."

WEAK (vague):
"My opponent doesn't care about working families."

STRONG (specific):
"My opponent voted three times against raising the minimum wage. 
I'll fight for a living wage because no one working full-time 
should struggle to pay rent in this community."
```

### Rules for Effective Contrast

```
1. Always be factual. Cite specific votes, statements, or actions.
2. Always include YOUR positive alternative. Contrast without a 
   positive close just makes both candidates look bad.
3. Let the facts do the work. Stating "My opponent voted against 
   veterans' benefits" is more powerful than "My opponent hates 
   veterans."
4. Test before deploying. If the contrast feels like a stretch or 
   requires too much explanation, it will not land with voters.
5. Do not punch down. If you are the frontrunner, contrast looks 
   desperate. If you are the challenger, contrast is expected.
```

## Testing Your Message

Before finalizing your positioning, test it:

```
1. THE KITCHEN TABLE TEST: If a voter heard your message once 
   while making dinner, would they remember one thing about you 
   tomorrow? If not, simplify.

2. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST: After every claim, ask "so what?" If 
   you cannot connect it to something voters care about in their 
   daily lives, cut it.

3. THE BUMPER STICKER TEST: Can you reduce your core message to 
   6-8 words? If not, it is too complex.

4. THE OPPONENT'S MOUTH TEST: Could your opponent say the exact 
   same thing? If yes, it is too generic. ("I'm for good schools 
   and safe streets" — everyone says this.) Add specificity.

5. THE VOLUNTEER TEST: Tell your message to five volunteers. 
   Ask them to repeat it back. If they cannot, simplify.
```
