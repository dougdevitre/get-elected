# Accessibility and Inclusion

A guide to making your campaign accessible to all voters, including people with disabilities, non-English speakers, and underserved communities. An inclusive campaign is not just the right thing to do -- it is smart strategy. Voters who feel seen and respected are more likely to support you, volunteer for you, and show up on election day.

---

## Why Accessibility Matters for Campaigns

- People with disabilities represent roughly 1 in 4 U.S. adults -- a significant voting bloc
- Multilingual communities are growing in every state
- Underserved communities often have the lowest voter turnout, representing untapped potential
- ADA compliance is a legal requirement for many campaign activities
- Inclusive campaigns earn trust and build broader coalitions

---

## ADA Compliance at Campaign Events

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and related state and local laws impose accessibility requirements on places of public accommodation. Campaign events held in public venues may be subject to these requirements. The guidance below provides general best practices. Consult an attorney if you have specific questions about your legal obligations. This guide is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.

### Venue Selection
- [ ] Choose venues that are wheelchair accessible (ramps, wide doorways, accessible restrooms)
- [ ] Verify accessible parking is available and clearly marked
- [ ] Confirm the venue has an accessible entrance (not just a side or back door)
- [ ] Check that the event space floor is navigable (no raised platforms without ramps, no tight aisles)
- [ ] Verify restrooms are ADA-compliant
- [ ] If the venue has a stage, ensure there is wheelchair access to the stage
- [ ] Visit the venue in advance to walk the accessibility path yourself

### Event Planning
- [ ] Include accessibility information on all event invitations ("This venue is wheelchair accessible. For accommodations, contact [email/phone].")
- [ ] Provide a way for attendees to request accommodations in advance
- [ ] Reserve seating near the front for people with mobility devices or hearing impairments
- [ ] Ensure adequate lighting for people who read lips or use sign language
- [ ] If serving food, offer options for common dietary restrictions and allergies
- [ ] Plan for service animals (they must be allowed in all areas open to the public)
- [ ] Train event volunteers on assisting attendees with disabilities (ask before helping; respect autonomy)

### Communication Access at Events
- [ ] Provide sign language interpreters for large events and any event where requested
- [ ] Use a microphone and sound system (do not rely on the speaker's unamplified voice)
- [ ] If using slides or video, include captions
- [ ] Provide printed materials in large print (16-18pt font minimum) upon request
- [ ] Consider assistive listening devices for large venues
- [ ] Face the audience when speaking (important for lip readers)

## Web and Digital Accessibility

Your website and digital content are often the first point of contact with voters. Make them accessible to everyone.

### Website Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 Guidelines)
- [ ] Use sufficient color contrast (minimum 4.5:1 ratio for normal text)
- [ ] Provide alt text for all images (describe the content, not just "photo")
- [ ] Ensure the site is fully navigable by keyboard (tab through all links, buttons, and forms)
- [ ] Use descriptive link text ("Read our education policy" not "Click here")
- [ ] Use heading hierarchy properly (H1, H2, H3 in order -- not just for styling)
- [ ] Ensure forms are labeled properly (screen readers need to know what each field is for)
- [ ] Do not use color as the only way to convey information
- [ ] Provide captions for all video content
- [ ] Provide transcripts for audio and video content
- [ ] Ensure the site is responsive and works on mobile devices
- [ ] Test the site with a screen reader (NVDA, VoiceOver, or JAWS)
- [ ] Run an automated accessibility check (WAVE, axe, or Lighthouse)

### Social Media Accessibility
- [ ] Add alt text to images on all platforms that support it (Facebook, X/Twitter, Instagram)
- [ ] Caption all video content (auto-captions are a start but should be reviewed for accuracy)
- [ ] Use CamelCase for hashtags (#GetOutTheVote not #getoutthevote) for screen reader compatibility
- [ ] Avoid using special characters or symbols in place of letters (they confuse screen readers)
- [ ] Limit emoji use in critical communications (screen readers read each emoji aloud)
- [ ] Provide image descriptions in the post text when alt text is not available

### Email Accessibility
- [ ] Use a clean, simple layout (avoid complex multi-column designs)
- [ ] Use adequate font sizes (14px minimum for body text)
- [ ] Include alt text on all images in emails
- [ ] Ensure links are descriptive and distinguishable from surrounding text
- [ ] Test emails in plain text mode to ensure content is still readable
- [ ] Provide a plain text version of every HTML email

## Multilingual Outreach and Language Access

### Identifying Language Needs
- [ ] Review Census data and voter file data for your district to identify language communities
- [ ] Determine which languages are most spoken after English in your district
- [ ] Identify precincts with high concentrations of non-English-speaking voters
- [ ] Check if your jurisdiction has any language access requirements for elections (the Voting Rights Act requires language assistance in some jurisdictions)

### Campaign Materials
- [ ] Translate core campaign materials into the primary non-English languages in your district
- [ ] At minimum, translate: campaign palm card, key issue positions, website landing page, and donation page
- [ ] Use professional translators -- do not rely solely on machine translation
- [ ] Have translations reviewed by a native speaker from the target community
- [ ] Include translated materials at all events and canvassing operations in multilingual areas
- [ ] Print bilingual materials where appropriate (English on one side, other language on the reverse)

### Canvassing and Phone Banking
- [ ] Recruit bilingual volunteers and assign them to multilingual precincts
- [ ] Prepare canvass and phone scripts in relevant languages
- [ ] Train all canvassers to greet voters in languages common to their turf (even a simple greeting shows respect)
- [ ] Provide language identification cards so canvassers can identify a voter's preferred language and connect them with a bilingual team member
- [ ] Track language preferences in your CRM for future contacts

### Events and Appearances
- [ ] Provide interpretation at events in multilingual communities (consecutive or simultaneous)
- [ ] Hire professional interpreters for formal events; bilingual volunteers can assist at informal gatherings
- [ ] Announce at the beginning of events that interpretation is available
- [ ] Provide printed materials in attendees' languages

## Reaching Underserved Communities

### Identifying Underserved Communities
- [ ] Low-income neighborhoods with historically low voter turnout
- [ ] Communities of color that have been underrepresented in local politics
- [ ] Rural areas with limited access to campaign events and information
- [ ] Senior communities, including assisted living and nursing facilities
- [ ] Native American and Indigenous communities
- [ ] Recently naturalized citizens
- [ ] Formerly incarcerated individuals (voting eligibility varies by state -- know your state's law)

### Engagement Strategies
- [ ] Go to the community -- do not expect them to come to you. Hold events in their neighborhoods, their churches, their community centers.
- [ ] Build relationships with trusted community leaders and organizations before asking for votes
- [ ] Listen first: attend community meetings, understand their priorities, and incorporate their concerns into your platform
- [ ] Hire staff and recruit volunteers who reflect the communities you are engaging
- [ ] Address specific barriers to voting (transportation, registration, ID requirements) with concrete solutions
- [ ] Follow through on commitments -- underserved communities are accustomed to being courted and then ignored

### Voter Registration
- [ ] Organize voter registration drives in underserved areas
- [ ] Partner with community organizations for registration events
- [ ] Train volunteers on voter registration rules for your state
- [ ] Follow up with newly registered voters to keep them engaged through election day

## Disability Inclusion Beyond Compliance

Meeting minimum ADA requirements is a starting point, not the finish line. Go further.

- [ ] Include people with disabilities in your campaign staff and volunteer base
- [ ] Feature disability issues in your platform (healthcare, employment, transportation, housing, education)
- [ ] Consult with disability advocacy organizations in your district
- [ ] Include people with disabilities in campaign photos, videos, and media (with their consent)
- [ ] Address disability issues in candidate forums and debates
- [ ] Consider appointing a disability inclusion advisor to your campaign

### Specific Disability Considerations
- **Mobility impairments:** Ensure all venues and events are physically accessible. Offer seating at all standing events.
- **Visual impairments:** Provide materials in large print and digital formats compatible with screen readers. Describe visual content verbally at events.
- **Hearing impairments:** Provide sign language interpreters, captions, and written materials. Face the audience when speaking.
- **Cognitive and intellectual disabilities:** Use plain language in materials. Avoid jargon. Provide easy-to-read summaries of complex positions.
- **Mental health conditions:** Create welcoming, low-pressure environments. Train volunteers to be patient and non-judgmental.

## Accessible Voting Information

Help every voter understand how to exercise their right to vote.

- [ ] Publish clear, simple voting instructions in multiple languages and accessible formats
- [ ] Include information on accessible voting equipment and procedures at polling places
- [ ] Provide information on curbside voting options for people who cannot enter polling places
- [ ] Publicize the right to assistance at the polls (most jurisdictions allow a voter to bring someone to help them)
- [ ] Ensure your GOTV operations (see `gotv-plan.md`) include accessibility: accessible ride-to-polls vehicles, outreach to assisted living facilities, etc.

## Key Principles

1. **Accessibility is not an afterthought.** Build it into every plan from the start, not as a last-minute addition.
2. **Ask, do not assume.** Ask people what accommodations they need rather than guessing. Different people have different needs.
3. **Nothing about us without us.** Include people from the communities you are trying to reach in the planning process.
4. **Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.** Meeting legal minimums is necessary but not sufficient. Aim higher.
5. **Every voter matters.** A campaign that excludes any group of voters is leaving votes -- and trust -- on the table.
