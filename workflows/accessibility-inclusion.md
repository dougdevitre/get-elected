# Accessibility and Inclusion

Making your campaign accessible to all voters -- people with disabilities, non-English speakers, and underserved communities. Inclusive campaigning is both the right thing to do and smart strategy. Voters who feel seen and respected are more likely to support you, volunteer for you, and show up on election day.

---

## Why Accessibility Matters

- People with disabilities represent roughly 1 in 4 U.S. adults -- a massive, underserved voting bloc
- Multilingual communities are growing in every state and every district
- Underserved communities have the lowest turnout, representing untapped potential
- ADA compliance is a legal requirement for many campaign activities
- Inclusive campaigns build broader coalitions and earn lasting trust

---

## ADA Compliance at Campaign Events

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and related state and local laws impose accessibility requirements on places of public accommodation. Campaign events held in public venues may be subject to these requirements. Consult an attorney for specific legal questions. This guide is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.

### Venue Accessibility Checklist
- [ ] Wheelchair accessible entrance (main entrance, not side or back door)
- [ ] Ramps and/or elevators to all event areas
- [ ] Wide doorways and aisles (minimum 36 inches)
- [ ] Accessible parking clearly marked and close to entrance
- [ ] ADA-compliant restrooms
- [ ] Wheelchair access to stage or speaking area
- [ ] Accessible path from parking to entrance to seating to restrooms
- [ ] Service animal accommodations (allowed in all public areas)
- [ ] Adequate lighting throughout (critical for lip readers and sign language users)

### Accommodations Process
- [ ] Include accommodation language on every invitation: "For accessibility accommodations, contact [email/phone] at least 48 hours before the event."
- [ ] Provide a clear, simple way to request accommodations (email, phone, web form)
- [ ] Designate one person to receive and fulfill accommodation requests
- [ ] Common accommodations to prepare for: sign language interpreters, large-print materials, reserved seating, assistive listening devices, wheelchair-accessible seating

### Signage and Wayfinding
- [ ] Post accessible entrance signs visible from the street and parking area
- [ ] Use high-contrast, large-font signage (minimum 24pt)
- [ ] Include directional signs for accessible routes, restrooms, and exits
- [ ] Place signs at eye level for wheelchair users (48 inches from floor)
- [ ] Use both text and universal symbols (wheelchair symbol, hearing loop symbol)

## Multilingual Outreach

### Identifying Language Needs
- [ ] Review Census data for your district to identify language communities
- [ ] Analyze voter file data for non-English language preferences
- [ ] Identify precincts with high concentrations of non-English speakers
- [ ] Check if your jurisdiction has Voting Rights Act language assistance requirements
- [ ] Determine which 2-3 languages (beyond English) are most spoken in your district

### Translated Campaign Materials
**Priority order for translation:**
1. Campaign palm card / door hanger (highest volume distribution)
2. Website landing page and key issue pages
3. Donation page
4. Canvass and phone scripts
5. Key issue position summaries
6. Social media posts (at least 2-3 per week in each language)
7. Email blasts to identified non-English-preference voters

**Translation standards:**
- [ ] Use professional human translators -- not machine translation alone
- [ ] Have every translation reviewed by a native speaker from the local community
- [ ] Use culturally appropriate phrasing, not just literal translation
- [ ] Print bilingual materials where practical (English on one side, other language on reverse)

### Bilingual Volunteers
- [ ] Actively recruit bilingual volunteers through community organizations, churches, cultural centers
- [ ] Assign bilingual canvassers to multilingual precincts
- [ ] Prepare canvass and phone scripts in each target language
- [ ] Train all canvassers to greet voters in the community's primary language (even a simple hello)
- [ ] Provide language identification cards so monolingual canvassers can identify a voter's language and connect them to a bilingual team member

### Community Partnerships
- [ ] Build relationships with cultural organizations, ethnic media outlets, and community leaders
- [ ] Attend community events and cultural celebrations (listen first, then participate)
- [ ] Partner with community organizations for voter registration drives
- [ ] Offer your campaign space for community meetings and events
- [ ] Hire staff who reflect the linguistic diversity of your district

## Digital Accessibility (WCAG Compliance)

### Website (WCAG 2.1 AA Standard)
- [ ] Color contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text
- [ ] Alt text on every image (descriptive, not just "photo" -- describe what is happening)
- [ ] Full keyboard navigation (tab through all links, buttons, forms without a mouse)
- [ ] Descriptive link text ("Read our education plan" not "Click here")
- [ ] Proper heading hierarchy (H1, H2, H3 in logical order)
- [ ] Labeled form fields (screen readers need to know what each field is)
- [ ] No information conveyed by color alone
- [ ] Captions on all video content
- [ ] Transcripts for all audio and video
- [ ] Responsive design for mobile and tablet
- [ ] Skip-navigation links for screen reader users
- [ ] No auto-playing audio or video
- [ ] Test with a screen reader (NVDA, VoiceOver, or JAWS)
- [ ] Run automated checks (WAVE, axe, or Lighthouse)

### Social Media Accessibility
- [ ] Alt text on all images (every platform now supports this)
- [ ] Captions on all video (review auto-captions for accuracy)
- [ ] CamelCase hashtags (#GetOutTheVote not #getoutthevote) for screen reader compatibility
- [ ] Avoid special characters or symbols replacing letters (confuses screen readers)
- [ ] Limit emoji in critical communications (screen readers read each one aloud)
- [ ] Provide image descriptions in post text when alt text is unavailable

### Email Accessibility
- [ ] Clean single-column layout (avoid complex multi-column designs)
- [ ] Minimum 14px font for body text
- [ ] Alt text on all images
- [ ] Descriptive, distinguishable links
- [ ] Provide a plain-text version of every HTML email
- [ ] Test in plain text mode to verify readability

### Screen Reader Compatibility
- [ ] Use semantic HTML (buttons for actions, links for navigation, headings for structure)
- [ ] Label all interactive elements (buttons, form fields, dropdowns)
- [ ] Ensure dynamic content updates are announced to screen readers (ARIA live regions)
- [ ] Test forms with screen readers -- donation forms and volunteer sign-ups are critical

## Language Access Planning

### Create a Language Access Plan
1. **Assess:** Identify the languages spoken in your district and the size of each language community
2. **Prioritize:** Focus on the top 2-3 non-English languages by population
3. **Resource:** Budget for translation, interpretation, and bilingual staffing
4. **Implement:** Translate materials, recruit bilingual volunteers, partner with community organizations
5. **Monitor:** Track engagement by language community and adjust outreach accordingly

### Language Access at Events
- [ ] Provide interpretation at events in multilingual communities (consecutive or simultaneous)
- [ ] Hire professional interpreters for formal events; bilingual volunteers for informal ones
- [ ] Announce at the start of events that interpretation is available
- [ ] Provide printed materials in attendees' primary languages
- [ ] Display multilingual welcome signage at event entrances

## Disability Inclusion in Staff and Volunteers

Beyond compliance -- actively include people with disabilities in your campaign.

- [ ] Recruit people with disabilities for staff and volunteer roles
- [ ] Ensure your campaign office is physically accessible
- [ ] Provide reasonable accommodations for staff and volunteers with disabilities
- [ ] Feature disability policy in your platform (healthcare, employment, transportation, housing, education)
- [ ] Consult with disability advocacy organizations in your district
- [ ] Include people with disabilities in campaign photos and videos (with consent)
- [ ] Appoint a disability inclusion advisor to the campaign
- [ ] Use person-first or identity-first language based on community preference

### Specific Disability Considerations
- **Mobility:** Accessible venues, seating at standing events, accessible campaign office
- **Visual:** Large-print materials, screen-reader-compatible digital content, verbal descriptions at events
- **Hearing:** Sign language interpreters, captions, written materials, face the audience when speaking
- **Cognitive:** Plain language materials, avoid jargon, easy-to-read summaries of positions
- **Mental health:** Welcoming low-pressure environments, patient and non-judgmental volunteer training

## Reaching Underserved Communities

### Identify Target Communities
- [ ] Low-income neighborhoods with historically low turnout
- [ ] Communities of color underrepresented in local politics
- [ ] Rural areas with limited access to campaign events and information
- [ ] Senior communities, including assisted living facilities
- [ ] Native American and Indigenous communities
- [ ] Recently naturalized citizens
- [ ] Formerly incarcerated individuals (voting eligibility varies by state)
- [ ] Unhoused populations (may have voting rights -- check your state)

### Engagement Principles
- [ ] Go to the community -- hold events in their neighborhoods, churches, community centers
- [ ] Build relationships with trusted leaders before asking for votes
- [ ] Listen first: attend community meetings, understand priorities, incorporate concerns into your platform
- [ ] Hire staff and recruit volunteers who reflect these communities
- [ ] Address specific voting barriers: transportation, registration, ID requirements
- [ ] Follow through on commitments -- underserved communities are accustomed to being courted and then ignored
- [ ] Organize voter registration drives in underserved areas
- [ ] Partner with community organizations for outreach

## Key Principles

1. **Build accessibility in from the start.** It is easier and cheaper than retrofitting.
2. **Ask, do not assume.** Let people tell you what they need.
3. **Nothing about us without us.** Include the communities you serve in your planning.
4. **Compliance is the floor.** Legal minimums are the starting point, not the goal.
5. **Every voter matters.** A campaign that excludes any group leaves votes and trust on the table.
