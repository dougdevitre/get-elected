# Fundraising Plan

A strategic framework for raising the money your campaign needs to win. Fundraising is the single most important candidate activity -- it funds everything else. This guide covers goal-setting, source allocation, call time, events, online fundraising, and major donor cultivation.

---

## Fundraising Goal Calculation

### How Much Do You Need to Raise?

- [ ] Research what winning campaigns in comparable races have spent (last 2-3 cycles)
- [ ] Review your campaign plan budget (see `campaign-plan-builder.md`)
- [ ] Set a total fundraising goal that covers your budget plus a 10% contingency
- [ ] Break the total into monthly fundraising targets
- [ ] Front-load targets: you should raise 50% of your goal in the first 60% of the campaign

### Monthly Target Template

| Month 3 | $____ | $____ | $____ | $____ |

| Final Month | $____ | $____ (= total goal) | $____ | $____ |

## Source Allocation

Not all money comes from the same place. Diversify your fundraising sources to reduce risk.

### Typical Source Breakdown

| Source | Small Local Race | Mid-Size Race | Large Race |
|---|---|---|---|
| Candidate personal contribution | 10-30% | 5-15% | 0-10% |
| Personal network (friends/family) | 25-40% | 15-25% | 10-15% |
| Individual major donors ($200+) | 10-20% | 20-30% | 25-35% |
| Small dollar / grassroots (under $200) | 5-15% | 10-20% | 15-25% |
| Events | 10-20% | 10-15% | 5-10% |
| PACs / organizations | 0-10% | 5-15% | 10-20% |
| Party committees | 0-5% | 0-10% | 0-10% |
| Online fundraising | 5-15% | 10-20% | 15-25% |

- [ ] Set a target dollar amount for each source category
- [ ] Identify your strongest source and plan to maximize it
- [ ] Identify your weakest source and develop a plan to grow it
- [ ] Never depend on a single source for more than 40% of your budget

## Call Time: The Core Fundraising Activity

Call time is the candidate personally calling potential donors to ask for money. It is the single most effective fundraising method. Most candidates hate it. Do it anyway.

### Call Time Setup

- [ ] Block 1-2 hours of call time on the candidate's calendar every day (non-negotiable)
- [ ] Set up a quiet room with a phone, the call list, and a tracker
- [ ] Prepare a call list ranked by likelihood and capacity to give
- [ ] Write a call script (see below) and practice it until it feels natural
- [ ] Assign a call time manager to sit with the candidate and keep them on track
- [ ] Set a daily call goal (15-25 calls per hour is a good pace)
- [ ] Track results: calls made, contacts reached, asks made, pledges received, money raised

### Call Script Framework

1. **Greeting:** "Hi [Name], this is [Candidate]. Do you have a couple of minutes?"
2. **Connection:** Reference your relationship or a shared interest (30 seconds max)
3. **Why you are running:** Your core message in 2-3 sentences
4. **The ask:** "I am calling to ask for your support. Can I count on you for $[specific amount]?"
5. **Handle the response:** If yes, provide instructions. If maybe, set a follow-up date. If no, thank them and ask if they can help in other ways.
6. **Close:** Thank them regardless of the answer. Ask if they can suggest others to call.

### Call Time Coaching Tips

- Always ask for a specific dollar amount (never "whatever you can give")
- Ask for more than you expect -- people negotiate down, not up
- Silence after the ask is okay -- let them respond
- Track pledges separately from received money -- follow up on every pledge
- Keep the energy positive; take short breaks if frustration sets in
- The candidate must make the calls -- staff calls are far less effective
- After each session, immediately send follow-up emails with donation links

## The Ask Ladder

Not every donor is ready for the same ask. Build relationships progressively.

### Ladder Stages

| Stage | Action | Typical Amount |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Awareness | Introduce the campaign, share your story | No ask |
| 2. Small ask | Invite to an event, ask for a small contribution | $25-$100 |
| 3. Mid-level ask | Personal call or meeting, specific project funding | $100-$500 |
| 4. Major ask | One-on-one meeting, detailed campaign briefing | $500-max |
| 5. Max out | Ask for maximum contribution | Legal limit |
| 6. Bundler | Ask them to raise money from their network | Varies |

- [ ] Classify your prospect list by current ladder stage
- [ ] Create a plan to move each prospect up at least one rung
- [ ] Invest the most personal time in major donor prospects (Stage 3-6)

## Fundraising Events

Events raise money and build community. They range from house parties to formal dinners.

### Event Types

- **House party** ($25-$100 suggested donation): host opens their home, candidate speaks, light refreshments. Low cost, high relationship value.
- **Meet and greet** ($50-$250): similar to house party but slightly more formal. Focus on networking.
- **Fundraising reception** ($100-$500): larger venue, program with speakers, often tied to an endorsement or milestone.
- **Major donor dinner** ($500-max): small group, intimate, candidate has extended conversation time.
- **Online event / virtual town hall** ($0-$100): low cost, broad reach, good for small-dollar fundraising.

### Event Checklist

- [ ] Recruit a host (someone who will invite their network and open their home/venue)
- [ ] Set the date, time, and suggested contribution amount
- [ ] Create an invitation (email, printed, or both)
- [ ] Set up an event page with online payment option
- [ ] Send invitations 3 weeks out; reminder at 1 week and 2 days
- [ ] Prepare the candidate's remarks (5-10 minutes, end with a clear ask)
- [ ] Collect contact information from every attendee
- [ ] Send thank-you notes within 48 hours to all attendees
- [ ] Follow up with anyone who attended but did not give

## Online and Grassroots Fundraising

### Online Fundraising

- [ ] Set up a donation page on your website (ActBlue, Anedot, WinRed, or similar)
- [ ] Ensure the page is mobile-friendly and loads quickly
- [ ] Include required legal disclaimers on the donation page
- [ ] Set suggested donation amounts (e.g., $10, $25, $50, $100, $250, Other)
- [ ] Create compelling fundraising emails with clear asks and deadlines
- [ ] Send fundraising emails 2-3 times per week during active periods
- [ ] Use urgency triggers: deadlines, matching gifts, opponent announcements
- [ ] A/B test subject lines and send times

### Grassroots Fundraising

- [ ] Identify issue-based communities that align with your platform
- [ ] Build an email list through events, canvassing, and social media
- [ ] Share your story authentically -- grassroots donors give to people, not campaigns
- [ ] Set up recurring donation options (monthly giving)
- [ ] Thank small donors personally when possible -- they become your most loyal supporters

## Major Donor Cultivation

Major donors (typically $500+ or max-out contributors) require a personal cultivation process.

### Cultivation Process

1. **Identify:** Research potential major donors (personal network, donor databases, event attendees)
2. **Qualify:** Determine their capacity and interest (do they give to political campaigns? at what level?)
3. **Cultivate:** Build a relationship -- meetings, calls, events, issue briefings
4. **Ask:** Make a specific, personal ask at the right moment
5. **Steward:** Thank them, keep them informed, make them feel invested in the campaign

- [ ] Build a major donor prospect list of at least 50 names
- [ ] Research each prospect's giving history and interests
- [ ] Assign each prospect a cultivation timeline
- [ ] Schedule one-on-one meetings or calls with top prospects
- [ ] After each interaction, record notes and plan the next step
- [ ] Send personalized updates to major donors (not just mass emails)

## Fundraising Compliance Reminders

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** Contribution limits, reporting requirements, and solicitation rules vary by jurisdiction. Ensure all fundraising activity complies with applicable law. See `donation-intake.md` for contribution processing procedures and `treasurer-setup.md` for financial compliance.

- [ ] Include "Paid for by [Committee Name]" on all solicitations
- [ ] Collect required donor information (name, address, employer, occupation) at time of contribution
- [ ] Do not accept contributions above the legal limit
- [ ] Do not accept contributions from prohibited sources
- [ ] Report all contributions according to your filing calendar
- [ ] Keep records of all fundraising solicitations and event materials

## Weekly Fundraising Review

Every week, review these metrics with your finance team:

- [ ] Total raised this week and month-to-date
- [ ] Number of new donors acquired
- [ ] Call time hours completed vs. target
- [ ] Pledges outstanding (follow up on all)
- [ ] Upcoming events and their projected revenue
- [ ] Cash on hand vs. upcoming expenses
- [ ] Progress against monthly and total fundraising goals

Fundraising is a discipline, not an event. Consistent daily effort -- especially call time -- will always outperform sporadic bursts of activity.
