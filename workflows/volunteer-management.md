# Volunteer Management

A guide to recruiting, training, deploying, and retaining the volunteers who power your campaign. In most campaigns -- especially at the local level -- volunteers are the difference between winning and losing. Treat them as your most valuable asset, because they are.

---

## Volunteer Recruitment

### Where to Find Volunteers
- [ ] Your personal network (friends, family, neighbors, colleagues)
- [ ] Campaign website volunteer signup page
- [ ] Social media calls to action
- [ ] Campaign events (every event should include a volunteer signup sheet)
- [ ] Door-to-door canvassing (ask supporters if they want to help)
- [ ] Community organizations aligned with your platform
- [ ] Local party committees and clubs
- [ ] Labor unions and professional associations
- [ ] College and university student groups
- [ ] Faith-based communities
- [ ] Previous campaign volunteers (if applicable)
- [ ] Online volunteer matching platforms

### Recruitment Best Practices
- [ ] Ask everyone for help -- you will be surprised who says yes
- [ ] Make the ask specific: "Can you knock doors for two hours this Saturday?" is better than "Can you help out sometime?"
- [ ] Follow up within 24 hours of someone expressing interest
- [ ] Have a volunteer signup form at every event and interaction (paper and digital)
- [ ] Collect: name, email, phone, address, availability, skills, and interests
- [ ] Set a recruitment goal (e.g., 50 volunteers by Month 3, 100 by Month 6)

## Volunteer Onboarding

First impressions matter. A volunteer's first experience with your campaign determines whether they come back.

### Welcome Process
- [ ] Send a welcome email within 24 hours of signup (thank them, tell them what to expect)
- [ ] Invite them to an orientation session (in-person or virtual)
- [ ] Provide a campaign overview: who the candidate is, what the campaign is about, the theory of victory
- [ ] Explain how volunteers fit into the plan (they are essential, not optional)
- [ ] Give them a first assignment immediately (even if small)
- [ ] Assign a volunteer captain or point of contact

### Orientation Agenda (30-60 minutes)
1. Welcome and introductions
2. Candidate story and campaign message (5 minutes)
3. Overview of the race and path to victory (5 minutes)
4. Volunteer roles and opportunities (10 minutes)
5. Logistics: schedules, locations, communication channels (5 minutes)
6. Q&A (5-10 minutes)
7. Sign up for first shift before leaving

## Volunteer Training

Different roles require different training. Invest time upfront -- well-trained volunteers are more effective and more likely to stay.

### Door-to-Door Canvassing Training
- [ ] Walk through the script line by line
- [ ] Practice with role-playing (have volunteers knock on each other's "doors")
- [ ] Explain the walk list and how to record responses
- [ ] Cover safety: always canvass during daylight, never enter a home, carry a charged phone, canvass in pairs when possible
- [ ] Explain what to do with hostile or confrontational voters (disengage politely, move on)
- [ ] Demonstrate the MiniVAN or canvassing app if using one
- [ ] Send them out with a buddy on their first shift

### Phone Banking Training
- [ ] Walk through the phone script
- [ ] Practice with role-playing (common responses and objections)
- [ ] Explain the phone system or dialer
- [ ] Cover how to record call results
- [ ] Set expectations: most calls go to voicemail, and that is normal
- [ ] Teach active listening techniques

### Event Volunteering Training
- [ ] Explain the event logistics and the volunteer's specific role
- [ ] Cover greeting and sign-in procedures
- [ ] Explain how to handle questions about the candidate or issues (keep it simple; direct complex questions to campaign staff)
- [ ] Review any fundraising asks that will happen at the event

### Data Entry Training
- [ ] Demonstrate the CRM or data system
- [ ] Walk through data entry procedures step by step
- [ ] Emphasize accuracy and completeness
- [ ] Explain the importance of data to the campaign's strategy

## Volunteer Roles

Match volunteers to roles that fit their skills and interests. Not everyone wants to knock doors.

| Role | Description | Skills Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Canvasser | Door-to-door voter contact | Comfortable talking to strangers |
| Phone banker | Telephone voter contact | Good phone manner, patience |
| Event volunteer | Setup, greeting, logistics at events | Organized, friendly |
| Data entry | Entering canvass results and donor data | Detail-oriented, computer skills |
| Social media helper | Creating or sharing content | Social media savvy |
| Yard sign crew | Delivering and placing yard signs | Vehicle, physical ability |
| Office volunteer | Administrative tasks, mailings | Organized, reliable |
| Driver | Transporting candidate, volunteers, or voters | Valid license, vehicle |
| House party host | Hosting campaign events at their home | Willing to invite their network |
| Volunteer captain | Managing a team of 5-10 volunteers | Leadership, reliability |

- [ ] Maintain a skills inventory for each volunteer
- [ ] Offer multiple entry points so everyone can contribute
- [ ] Allow volunteers to try different roles and find their fit

## Shift Management

### Scheduling
- [ ] Create a regular weekly schedule of volunteer shifts (canvass, phones, office)
- [ ] Publish the schedule at least one week in advance
- [ ] Use a signup tool (SignUpGenius, Google Sheets, MobilizeAmerica, or similar)
- [ ] Send reminders 48 hours and 2 hours before each shift
- [ ] Confirm attendance by text or call the morning of the shift
- [ ] Have backup plans for no-shows (over-recruit by 20-30%)

### During the Shift
- [ ] Start on time with a brief (5-minute) energy check-in
- [ ] Provide all necessary materials (walk lists, scripts, water, clipboards, literature)
- [ ] Have a shift leader present to answer questions and manage logistics
- [ ] Check in mid-shift to address problems
- [ ] End on time -- respect your volunteers' commitments
- [ ] Debrief at the end: what went well, what did you hear from voters, any issues?

### After the Shift
- [ ] Collect all walk lists, data, and materials
- [ ] Enter data into the CRM within 24 hours
- [ ] Send a thank-you message to every volunteer who showed up
- [ ] Note volunteers who excelled or who struggled (for follow-up)
- [ ] Invite them to sign up for the next shift

## Volunteer Recognition and Retention

Volunteers who feel appreciated come back. Volunteers who feel taken for granted do not.

### Recognition Strategies
- [ ] Thank volunteers personally and by name -- every time
- [ ] Public shout-outs on social media (with their permission)
- [ ] Volunteer of the week/month recognition
- [ ] Handwritten thank-you notes from the candidate
- [ ] Small tokens of appreciation (campaign buttons, stickers, t-shirts)
- [ ] Invite top volunteers to special events or strategy briefings
- [ ] Share impact data: "Your canvassing team knocked 500 doors this week!"

### Retention Strategies
- [ ] Make volunteering social and fun (food, music, team energy)
- [ ] Give volunteers increasing responsibility as they demonstrate commitment
- [ ] Promote reliable volunteers to team lead or captain roles
- [ ] Ask for feedback: "How is your experience? What can we improve?"
- [ ] Never waste their time -- have them doing meaningful work, not sitting idle
- [ ] Be flexible with schedules -- people have jobs, families, and other commitments
- [ ] Communicate regularly about the campaign's progress (they are invested in the outcome)

### Common Reasons Volunteers Quit
- They felt unappreciated or ignored
- Their time was wasted (poor organization, no meaningful work)
- They were asked to do something they were uncomfortable with
- Communication was poor (they did not know when or where to show up)
- The environment was negative, disorganized, or unwelcoming

## Legal Considerations

> **EDUCATIONAL DISCLAIMER:** Volunteer activity in campaigns is subject to certain legal rules. The information below provides general guidance. Consult a campaign finance attorney for specific questions. This guide is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.

- [ ] Volunteer time is NOT a campaign contribution (it is explicitly exempt under federal law and most state laws)
- [ ] However, if a volunteer's employer pays them to work on the campaign, that IS a contribution from the employer
- [ ] Volunteers who use their own money for campaign expenses (gas, supplies) may be making in-kind contributions -- establish a reimbursement policy
- [ ] Volunteers who are paid anything (stipends, per diems) may be considered staff, with corresponding employment law and reporting obligations
- [ ] Minors can volunteer with parental consent, but be aware of child labor laws and safety
- [ ] Volunteers cannot be required to make contributions as a condition of volunteering
- [ ] International visitors on certain visas may volunteer if they are not compensated, but rules are complex -- verify before accepting
- [ ] Maintain a volunteer contact list with consent for campaign communications (comply with applicable texting and calling laws)

## Volunteer Management Tools

- **Signup and scheduling:** SignUpGenius, MobilizeAmerica, Google Sheets
- **Communication:** Slack, Signal, GroupMe, text/SMS platforms (Hustle, ThruText)
- **Data and canvassing:** VAN/VoteBuilder MiniVAN, Organizer, Reach
- **Phone banking:** ThruTalk, LiveVox, OpenVPB
- **Recognition tracking:** Simple spreadsheet tracking shifts completed per volunteer

## Key Principles

1. **Treat volunteers like gold.** They are giving you their most precious resource: their time.
2. **Be organized.** Nothing drives volunteers away faster than showing up and having nothing to do.
3. **Make it social.** People volunteer for the cause, but they come back for the community.
4. **Start small, grow steadily.** Five committed volunteers who come back every week are more valuable than fifty who come once.
5. **Every volunteer is a potential leader.** Identify and develop your best volunteers into team captains and future campaign leaders.
