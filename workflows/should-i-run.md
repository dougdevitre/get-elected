# Should I Run? Decision Framework

A structured framework for evaluating whether you should run for office. This is the most important decision you will make in your campaign -- the decision to begin. Use this guide honestly. The goal is not to talk you into or out of running, but to help you make an informed choice.

---

## How to Use This Framework

Score each section on a 1-5 scale based on the criteria provided. At the end, tally your scores and review the assessment ranges. Be brutally honest -- it is better to discover a fatal flaw now than after you have raised money and made public commitments.

---

## Section 1: Personal Readiness

### 1A. Family and Personal Life

Running for office will consume your time, expose your life to public scrutiny, and put stress on your relationships. Before you begin, have an honest conversation with the people closest to you.

**Questions to answer:**
- Have you discussed this decision with your spouse/partner and immediate family?
- Do they support you running? Actively or reluctantly?
- Can your family handle the time commitment (evenings, weekends, months of effort)?
- Are there family health issues, caregiving responsibilities, or life events that would make this the wrong time?
- Can your family handle public scrutiny and potential attacks?
- Are children old enough to understand, or will they be affected?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Full family support, no major personal conflicts, good timing
- **4** - Family is supportive with some reservations; manageable timing
- **3** - Mixed support; some real timing concerns
- **2** - Significant family resistance or major personal conflicts
- **1** - Family opposed or personal circumstances make this clearly the wrong time

**Your score: ___**

### 1B. Financial Readiness

Campaigns cost money -- both the campaign itself and the personal financial impact on you.

**Questions to answer:**
- Can you sustain your household income during the campaign?
- If the position is full-time, can you afford the salary (or lack thereof)?
- Will you need to leave or reduce your current employment?
- Do you have personal debts or financial obligations that could be weaponized?
- Can you personally contribute or loan money to the campaign to seed it?
- Are you prepared for the financial disclosure requirements of the office?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Financially stable, can absorb the impact, no concerning disclosures
- **4** - Manageable financial situation with some adjustment
- **3** - Will be tight; requires significant financial planning
- **2** - Real financial strain; could create hardship
- **1** - Financial situation is a serious barrier

**Your score: ___**

### 1C. Personal History ("Skeletons")

Anything in your past can and will be found. It is far better to identify issues now and prepare for them than to be surprised.

**Conduct a thorough self-assessment:**
- Criminal record (arrests, charges, convictions -- including expunged records)
- Civil lawsuits (as plaintiff or defendant)
- Tax issues (liens, late filings, audits)
- Bankruptcy or foreclosure
- Divorce or custody disputes
- Social media history (posts, photos, comments going back years)
- Professional discipline or licensing issues
- Academic issues (plagiarism, discipline)
- Substance abuse history
- Extramarital affairs or personal conduct issues
- Association with controversial individuals or organizations
- Business failures or investor disputes
- Statements on record that contradict your current positions

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Clean record; no significant vulnerabilities
- **4** - Minor issues that can be explained and are unlikely to be disqualifying
- **3** - Some issues that will require careful handling and preparation
- **2** - Significant vulnerabilities that could dominate the campaign narrative
- **1** - Disqualifying issues that would make a campaign non-viable

**Your score: ___**

### 1D. Temperament and Skills

Campaigning requires specific skills and temperament. Not everyone who would be a great officeholder is a great candidate.

**Honest self-assessment:**
- Are you comfortable asking people for money? (This is the #1 activity of candidates)
- Can you handle personal criticism and public attacks without losing composure?
- Are you comfortable speaking in public and in uncontrolled settings?
- Can you stay disciplined and on message for months?
- Are you willing to knock on doors and meet strangers?
- Can you manage a team and make decisions under pressure?
- Do you have the stamina for 12-16 hour days for months?
- Can you accept losing gracefully?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Natural campaigner; comfortable with all aspects
- **4** - Strong in most areas; can develop weaker areas
- **3** - Average; will need coaching and support in several areas
- **2** - Uncomfortable with multiple core campaign activities
- **1** - Fundamentally unsuited to the demands of campaigning

**Your score: ___**

## Section 2: Political Landscape

### 2A. Incumbency and Open Seats

The single biggest factor in electoral politics is whether there is an incumbent.

**Assessment:**
- Is the seat open (no incumbent running)?
- If there is an incumbent, what is their approval/favorability?
- Has the incumbent had any scandals, controversies, or significant policy failures?
- What is the incumbent's fundraising capacity?
- Has anyone successfully challenged an incumbent for this seat in recent history?
- Is the incumbent vulnerable due to redistricting or changing demographics?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Open seat with no dominant frontrunner
- **4** - Open seat with a competitive field, or vulnerable incumbent
- **3** - Incumbent with some weaknesses but still favored
- **2** - Popular incumbent with no major vulnerabilities
- **1** - Beloved incumbent with overwhelming support and resources

**Your score: ___**

### 2B. District/Electorate Lean

Understanding the partisan and ideological composition of your electorate is essential.

**Assessment:**
- What is the partisan registration breakdown of the district?
- How has the district voted in recent elections at your level and above?
- Are demographic trends moving in your direction?
- Is there a competitive primary, general, or both?
- What is the ideological center of the electorate you need to win?
- Does your party affiliation match the district lean for this office?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - District strongly favors your party/ideology; general election is the easier fight
- **4** - District leans your way; competitive but favorable
- **3** - True swing district; either party can win
- **2** - District leans against you; uphill but not impossible
- **1** - District is heavily against your party/ideology; very long odds

**Your score: ___**

### 2C. Primary Field

If you face a primary, assess the competitive dynamics.

**Assessment:**
- How many candidates are running or likely to run in the primary?
- Are any of them well-known, well-funded, or organizationally strong?
- Is there a party-establishment favorite?
- Do you have a clear lane (ideological, geographic, demographic)?
- Is there a risk of splitting the vote with a similar candidate?
- What is the expected primary turnout and your path to winning it?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - No primary opposition or very weak field
- **4** - One or two opponents with clear differentiation in your favor
- **3** - Competitive primary with no clear frontrunner
- **2** - Strong primary opponent(s) with significant advantages
- **1** - Dominant primary opponent makes your path nearly impossible

**Your score: ___**

## Section 3: Resource Potential

### 3A. Fundraising Capacity

Money is not everything, but you need enough to be competitive.

**Assessment:**
- How much do you need to raise to be competitive? (Research comparable races)
- How large is your personal network of potential donors?
- Can you raise 25% of your budget from your personal network alone?
- Are there aligned organizations, PACs, or party committees that may support you?
- Is there a grassroots fundraising base (issue-driven small donors)?
- Are you comfortable making fundraising calls daily?
- Do you have access to fundraising events and donor networks?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Clear path to raising a competitive budget; strong donor network
- **4** - Can likely raise enough with significant effort; decent network
- **3** - Fundraising will be a challenge but not impossible
- **2** - Limited fundraising network; unclear path to competitiveness
- **1** - No realistic fundraising path to a competitive campaign

**Your score: ___**

### 3B. Volunteer and Organizational Base

Campaigns need people, not just money.

**Assessment:**
- Do you have a core group of committed supporters who will work on the campaign?
- Are there organizations (labor, civic, advocacy) that would mobilize for you?
- Do you have relationships in the community that would translate to volunteers?
- Is there an existing party infrastructure you can tap into?
- Can you recruit a competent campaign manager and key staff?
- Do you have access to experienced political operatives for advice?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Strong volunteer base; organizational support lined up; experienced team available
- **4** - Good base of supporters; some organizational interest; can recruit a team
- **3** - Some supporters but need significant recruitment; limited organizational ties
- **2** - Small personal network; no organizational support; difficult team recruitment
- **1** - No volunteer base; no organizational connections; no experienced help available

**Your score: ___**

### 3C. Endorsement Prospects

Endorsements provide credibility, volunteers, and sometimes money.

**Assessment:**
- Are there elected officials who would endorse you?
- Would community leaders, clergy, or business leaders publicly support you?
- Are there organizations whose endorsement would be meaningful?
- Do you have relationships with local media editorial boards?
- Is the party establishment likely to support, oppose, or stay neutral?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Multiple significant endorsements likely; strong establishment support
- **4** - Several meaningful endorsements possible; neutral-to-positive establishment
- **3** - A few endorsements possible; establishment is neutral
- **2** - Few endorsements likely; establishment may oppose
- **1** - No significant endorsements in sight; active establishment opposition

**Your score: ___**

## Section 4: Issue Fit and Motivation

### 4A. Issue Alignment

Your priorities must match what the electorate cares about.

**Assessment:**
- Do your top 3 issues align with the top concerns of the electorate?
- Can you speak authentically and knowledgeably about local issues?
- Are your positions within the mainstream of your target electorate?
- Do you have any positions that are deal-breakers for significant voter blocs?
- Can you articulate why you are running in one compelling sentence?
- Is there a clear "why now" for your candidacy?

**Scoring:**
- **5** - Strong issue alignment; compelling narrative; clear "why now"
- **4** - Good alignment on most issues; solid narrative
- **3** - Partial alignment; some issues are liabilities
- **2** - Significant misalignment on key issues
- **1** - Fundamental disconnect between your priorities and the electorate's

**Your score: ___**

### 4B. Motivation Check

Why you are running matters -- it affects your resilience, your authenticity, and your ability to lead.

**Honest reflection:**
- Are you running to serve or to achieve personal status?
- Do you have specific things you want to accomplish in office?
- Are you running because you are the best candidate, or because no one else is?
- Will you be okay if you lose?
- Are you willing to compromise and work within a system?
- Is this the right office for your goals, or should you aim higher or lower?

This section is not scored. It is for your reflection. But if your answers reveal that your motivation is primarily ego, revenge, or stepping-stone ambition, reconsider.

### Assessment Ranges

- **45-55: Strong Candidate.** You have the fundamentals in place. Move to `first-30-days.md` and begin building your campaign.
- **35-44: Viable but Challenging.** You can win, but you will need to address weaknesses. Identify your lowest scores and develop plans to mitigate them before committing.
- **25-34: Uphill Battle.** Significant obstacles exist. Consider whether this is the right race, the right time, or the right office. You may want to build toward a future run.
- **Below 25: Reconsider.** The fundamentals are not in place for a competitive campaign. This does not mean you should never run -- it means not now, and not this race.

## Red Flags: Do Not Run If...

These are conditions that should cause you to seriously reconsider, regardless of your total score:

1. **Your family is opposed.** A campaign will strain even the strongest relationships. Running against your family's wishes is a recipe for personal disaster.

2. **You have an undisclosed legal or personal issue that would be disqualifying if made public.** It will come out. It always comes out.

3. **You cannot raise at least 50% of the minimum competitive budget.** Underfunded campaigns do not just lose; they demoralize supporters and burn bridges.

4. **You are running primarily to raise your profile for a different goal.** Voters and supporters can sense inauthenticity. It wastes their time and your credibility.

5. **You scored a 1 on any single category.** A single critical weakness can sink an otherwise strong candidacy. Address it first or choose a different path.

6. **You are unwilling to do call time.** Fundraising is the single most important candidate activity. If you will not do it, you cannot run a competitive campaign.

7. **The math does not work.** If the district lean, incumbency, and resource gap make victory essentially impossible, your energy and your supporters' resources are better invested elsewhere.

## Alternative Paths

If now is not the time, consider:

- **Run for a different office** where the landscape is more favorable
- **Build toward a future run** by serving on boards, volunteering, building name ID
- **Support another candidate** whose values align with yours
- **Work within organizations** (party, advocacy, nonprofit) to advance your goals
- **Run for an appointed position** (board, commission) to build experience and visibility

The best candidates are often those who ran before, or who spent years building the foundation before launching. There is no shame in waiting for the right time.
